Bharat, Matt, and Lisa,
 
The original ERP was 10 pages long and did not fully answer Seema's question. Before rewriting and shortening the paper, I wanted to give you a “Here’s what I am thinking” and get feedback. 
 
Original Question: Should we pursue ERP sales in the small provider space, including ambulatory?
Answer: Yes, but…
I've dug through the data, looking at our deal sheets, and talking to Austin Kunz, Matt Patterson, and Kyle Milligan about what's actually happening in the field. Should we pursue this? Yes, but… not the way we're doing it now. Right now, we're trying to sell to everybody—Critical Access Hospitals, small clinics, medium hospitals, ambulatory groups, ASCs. We haven't made any hard choices about where to focus and what solution suite should focus on what. The ambulatory space has some momentum and guidelines on selling to 200+ provider ambulatory sites (Meharry Medical College), but we don't have a clear strategy for the rest of the small provider market.
Where is the juice worth the squeeze:
Small hospitals with supply chain needs (Fusion), multi-site ambulatory groups (NetSuite), and Critical Access Hospitals (if we solve implementation costs). We need three things: 1. Clear focus on where Fusion plays versus NetSuite, 2. Lower implementation costs (from $100K to $50K or a lot less), and 3. Confirm our segment approach.
What we avoid: Single-site small clinics with no growth path, greenfield (non-Oracle Health EHR customers), and large hospitals (300+ beds), except as a defensive retention play.
What's the lever Seema could help to pull: Accelerate the Patient Accounting APIs from FY27 to FY26 Q4. If we build a plug-and-play connector between our EHR and any ERP, we cut implementation costs, unlocking the full ~2,000 customer base
 
Market Reality & Size of the Opportunity
The Total Addressable Market (TAM) for small-provider ERP is approximately $3.7B. Our realistic Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) is about $2.8B—providers Oracle can reach. We have ~2,000 Oracle Health EHR customers in the small provider space. Our 3-year Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM) is $100M-$125M in bookings (2026-2029). That's roughly 3.5-4.5% —a healthy capture rate for enterprise software. The clock is ticking. Epic is thought to be building a native ERP by 2027.
 
What Customers Need – How We Bring Focus to NetSuite vs Fusion
Here is how I'm thinking about our ~2,000 Oracle Health EHR customers. They're not all the same—they fall into three distinct stages.
Stage 1: "Just the Financials”: Imagine you're a Critical Access Hospital in rural Montana. You have 20 beds and maybe 50 employees. Medicare cost reports are killing you, and you're running financials in Excel or QuickBooks. You just need to survive and stay compliant. These folks need basics: a General Ledger that understands healthcare, AP automation, and a proper Chart of Accounts for Medicare reporting. That solves their problem.
Product question: Fusion ($25K-$50K/year) or NetSuite? Austin's had success with Fusion; NetSuite might be faster (60 days vs 6 months). We should pilot both.
Opportunity: ~500 CAH customers = $10M-$15M if we address implementation costs.
Stage 2: "Add Procurement”: A multi-site ambulatory group like Meharry—100+ providers across 5-10 locations. Financials are sorted, but you're buying supplies across all sites with no visibility. They need basic procurement visibility.
Product fit: NetSuite shines here for ambulatory (Meharry: $75K-$80K Year 1, 60 days, happy customer). For hospital-affiliated sites, Fusion makes sense in the long term.
Opportunity: Large ambulatory groups + multi-site ASCs = $40M-$70M.
Stage 3: "Full Supply Chain”: A 150-bed community hospital or 15-site ASC platform competing with larger health systems. When there's a recall on surgical instruments, you need to know which ORs have them. You need robust inventory management and tight GPO integration to achieve bulk pricing. NetSuite and Multiview can't do a healthcare-specific supply chain. Fusion's recall management, par inventory, and GPO integration are built in—that's our differentiator.
Product fit: Full Fusion suite ($70K-$100K/year software, $300K-$400K Year 1 with implementation). Yes, implementation is a barrier we still have to solve
Opportunity: Small hospitals + large ASC platforms = $50M-$70M.

Seema,
 
I’m sharing a short (4 pages) paper on our recommended ERP approach for small providers. To save you time, here are the key takeaways:
 
NetSuite is already a proven fit for the ambulatory segment. We have ~500 small provider customers today, particularly in physician groups and ASCs, plus an active pipeline.
Oracle’s ERP portfolio currently serves distinct segments:
Fusion serves large hospitals (300+ beds, >1,500 employees)
NetSuite serves physician groups and ASCs
We currently re-sell a competitor called MultiView (OCI-hosted) for critical access hospitals and others where Fusion/NetSuite aren’t viable
The growth opportunity is in small-to-mid community hospitals (150–300 beds, <1,500 employees). These hospitals are often underserved by lightweight ERPs (like MultiView) yet too cost-sensitive for Fusion as implemented today. We’d likely look to displace Multiview in certain segments. Workday is also competitive here.
Two possible plays:
NetSuite could “level up” with healthcare functionality and tighter EMR integrations, but this would require substantial new product development and blur our positioning (with NetSuite and Fusion potentially competing).
Fusion could “scale down” via a standardized, lighter-weight implementation model (“Fusion Lite”), reducing configuration and cost while preserving functionality and brand strength. Based on early analysis, Fusion Lite appears the more credible path.
Product enablers we recommend exploring: standardized EMR integrations, off-the-shelf configuration packages, bundled ERP + EHR offerings, and pricing guardrails (≤2.5% of NPR). These changes would expand reach, improve affordability, and strengthen our competitive position against Workday.
 
We’ve aligned on this framing with colleagues from the Fusion side and from NetSuite sales, but further conversations with product leadership will be essential to validate the path forward. Given Summit demands, we’ve been careful not to over-engage product yet, but recommend doing so next.
