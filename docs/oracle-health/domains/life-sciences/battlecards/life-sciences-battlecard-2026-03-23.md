No competitor with a comparable EHR-sourced dataset is positioned to exploit this faster. Oracle should be the reference vendor for "FDA-compliant EHR-based RWE" in every pharma regulatory affairs conversation in 2026.

Competitor AI platforms need to be connected to data — Oracle's data and AI arrive together.

## TOP OBJECTIONS & RESPONSES

### OBJECTION 1: "Medidata is stronger for clinical trials — they have 2,100 customers and 20+ years of trial data."

**Acknowledge**: Medidata has the largest installed base of EDC/CTMS customers and a historically earned position as the default standard for clinical trial data management. Their 20+ years of trial data powering AI Intelligent Trials is a real competitive moat.

**Reframe**: Medidata is the strongest at running trials *in isolation*. But trials do not happen in isolation anymore. FDA's RWE guidance, ICH E6(R3) endorsement of risk-based monitoring, and the growing use of EHR data for synthetic control arms and patient identification all require a vendor with both trial AND care delivery data. Medidata has no EHR footprint — they literally cannot solve the enrollment velocity problem (slowest, most expensive part of any trial) the way Oracle can via EHR-integrated patient matching.

**Proof point**: Oracle's Learning Health Network connects a trial sponsor to 143 health systems and 1B+ patient encounters for pre-trial cohort feasibility and real-time enrollment identification. Medidata cannot offer this regardless of their historical trial data volume.

**Close**: "Medidata is the best system for managing a trial after enrollment starts. Oracle is the best system for identifying who should be in that trial, enrolling them faster, and generating the evidence needed to expand the label after it ends. Which problem costs your organization more money — the paperwork inside the trial, or the 18 months of failing to enroll?"

---

### OBJECTION 2: "Veeva is purpose-built for life sciences — Oracle is an EHR in the clinical trial space."

**Acknowledge**: Veeva's cloud-native, life-sciences-specific unified suite is a legitimate competitive strength. Their "Enter once" data model is elegant and their regulatory/quality/commercial coverage is genuinely comprehensive for mid-market pharma and biotech.

**Reframe**: Oracle Life Sciences — Clinical One, CTMS, Argus, InForm — was built *before* the Cerner acquisition. Oracle has been a clinical trial technology vendor since the 1990s. The EHR (Cerner) is additive data leverage, not the origin of Oracle's life sciences capability. Veeva, conversely, started as a CRM company (Salesforce-based) and has expanded into clinical trials — they are the EHR-company-expanding-into-life-sciences story, in reverse.

**Reframe 2 (forward-looking)**: Veeva's unified suite works beautifully when all your data is inside Veeva. The moment a pharmaceutical company needs FDA-compliant RWE from a real-world care setting, wants to identify trial-eligible patients at a point of care, or needs to generate a synthetic control arm using longitudinal clinical records — Veeva hits a structural ceiling. Oracle does not.

**Proof point**: IQVIA's 2026 data shows that 62% of core life sciences applications still run on legacy platforms. The migration to cloud is happening in phases. Veeva wins the "greenfield biotech" deal. Oracle wins the enterprise pharma that needs a bridge from legacy infrastructure *and* a real-world data strategy.

**Close**: "Veeva is purpose-built for the life sciences workflow as it existed in 2015. Oracle is built for the life sciences workflow as it exists post-FDA's RWE guidance in 2026 — where EHR data is a regulatory asset, not just a care delivery tool."

---

### OBJECTION 3: "Oracle's legacy EHR isn't built for clinical research — it's a clinical delivery platform."

**Acknowledge**: This is a fair observation about where legacy Cerner EHR sits in users' mental models. Most clinicians interact with Cerner as a documentation and ordering tool, not a research platform.

**Reframe**: This objection conflates the product with the data. The EHR is the data generation engine — it produces the 57B+ clinical results and 1B+ patient encounters that flow into the Learning Health Network. Oracle builds on *top of* that data with purpose-built research tools: Health Data Intelligence for cohort identification, the Learning Health Network for cross-site research access, and the Life Sciences AI Data Platform for analytics and agentic intelligence. The EHR is the pipeline. The research platform sits above it.

**Technical proof**: Oracle's product roadmap (published via oracle.com) explicitly includes: "Enhance population AI use cases for clinical trial" and "Deliver reasoning capabilities over omics and imaging datasets for research within" the Health and Life Sciences platform. This is active, funded development — not a future roadmap aspiration.

**Close**: "No one says Google's search engine isn't built for advertising because you use it to look up directions. The EHR and the research platform are different layers of the same data asset. Would you like to see how AMCs in our Learning Health Network are using Cerner EHR data to identify trial-eligible patients in real time?"

### OBJECTION 4: "Oracle is too expensive — Veeva or Medidata are better value."

**Acknowledge**: Oracle's enterprise licensing and infrastructure costs have historically been higher than SaaS competitors. This is a real buyer perception, particularly in cost-conscious biotech environments.

**Reframe — TCO frame**: The total cost of a clinical trial program is not the CTMS or EDC license. It is enrollment delays ($600K+ per day of trial delay at Phase III scale), data reconciliation overhead between disconnected systems, and failed brand extensions because RWE was too expensive to generate. Oracle's integrated stack eliminates the reconciliation tax between EHR data, trial data, safety data, and commercial data. A single-vendor integrated approach has a lower total cost of ownership than best-of-breed point solutions requiring custom integration maintenance.

**Reframe — revenue frame for health systems**: For health systems considering joining the Learning Health Network, Oracle's model inverts the cost narrative: participation in sponsored trials generates revenue for the health system through Oracle's data monetization model. Oracle is not just a cost center — it is a revenue-generation platform for provider organizations in the research economy.

**Proof point**: Benchmark against the cost of running a Medidata + Veeva + IQVIA + a data integration layer simultaneously. The total vendor and integration cost in that scenario typically exceeds Oracle's integrated price.

**Close**: "What is the annual cost of manual data reconciliation between your EDC, CTMS, and safety systems today? What would your team work on if that overhead disappeared?"

   *(Listen for: any number above 15% or any admission of enrollment overruns. This is Oracle's EHR-integrated enrollment pitch territory.)*

   *(Listen for: 3+ vendors and any manual reconciliation. This opens the Oracle integrated stack conversation.)*

## COMPETITIVE TRAPS THEY'LL SET

### TRAP 1: "Show us your specialized clinical trial team and reference customers — you're an EHR company."

**What they're doing**: Forcing Oracle to defend clinical trial credentials against Medidata or Veeva's deeper trial-specialist brand identity. Attempting to reframe Oracle as an outsider in the clinical research market.

**The Counter**: Do not accept the premise. "Oracle has been in clinical trial technology since the 1990s. Oracle Clinical, InForm, Argus Safety, and CTMS predate the Cerner acquisition by decades. We have reference customers across global pharma including BeiGene for enterprise CTMS. The acquisition of Cerner added a real-world data asset to a clinical trial platform that already existed — it didn't create one." Then pivot to: "More importantly, the question isn't which vendor has the most trial-management references. The question is which vendor can help you solve the enrollment velocity and RWE generation problems that are costing you the most money right now. Let's talk about those."

---

### TRAP 2: "IQVIA already has AI agents running in our workflows — why would we replace them with Oracle?"

**What they're doing**: Using IQVIA's installed footprint (19 of top 20 pharma) as a "we're already sorted on AI" brush-off. Trying to position the AI conversation as closed.

**The Counter**: Do not compete with IQVIA.ai on agent count. Compete on data provenance. "IQVIA's agents are excellent for commercial analytics and operational efficiency. We're not asking you to replace them for those use cases. We are asking: when your regulatory affairs team needs to build a synthetic control arm or generate EHR-based RWE for an FDA submission, what data do those IQVIA agents run on? IQVIA data is primarily claims and commercial data. Oracle's 129 million longitudinal clinical records include the lab values, vital trends, and physician assessments that FDA considers high-quality clinical evidence. You don't need to choose — you need the right data for the right submission type. Oracle is the right data for the RWE clinical evidence use case." The key: IQVIA and Oracle can coexist. Oracle owns the RWE-for-submissions lane; IQVIA owns commercial analytics. Do not force a replacement conversation unless the customer volunteers it.
