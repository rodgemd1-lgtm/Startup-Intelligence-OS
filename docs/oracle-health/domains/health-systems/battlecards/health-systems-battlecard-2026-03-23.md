## WHY ORACLE WINS IN HEALTH SYSTEMS

- **The only true full-stack healthcare platform.** Oracle is the only vendor where the EHR, the clinical database (Oracle DB), the analytics infrastructure, the AI layer, and the supply chain/ERP system are engineered as a single governed platform on OCI. Epic is a great EHR. Azure is a great cloud. Neither is a platform. Oracle is. Proof: VA/DoD deployments at scale -- the most security-intensive healthcare environment in the world chose Oracle.

- **Ground-up AI architecture, not AI grafted onto legacy.** Oracle Health's new EHR was built from scratch on OCI with a voice-first clinical AI agent at the core. Epic's AI is layered onto 30 years of accumulated architecture. Oracle's semantic AI foundation allows health systems to extend Oracle's agents, build their own, and integrate third-party models -- something no other vendor offers natively. Proof: ONC Health IT certified; DEA compliant for ePrescribing of controlled substances at launch.

- **Enterprise-grade at federal scale, community-grade in price flexibility.** Oracle's federal government track record (VA, DoD, Indian Health Service) provides proof of security, scalability, and multi-site complexity management that no commercial competitor can demonstrate. Simultaneously, Oracle's legacy Cerner presence across rural and community hospitals means the upgrade path for smaller systems is intra-family -- not a rip-and-replace. Proof: 9 additional VA medical centers deploying Oracle Health EHR in 2026.

- **Revenue cycle ROI is measurable and immediate.** In a market where hospital operating margins averaged -0.6% in January 2026, Oracle's AI revenue cycle capabilities -- claim denial management, electronic prior auth, ambient coding -- deliver 90-day quantifiable ROI. Oracle can underwrite the EHR deal on revenue cycle savings alone in many mid-market accounts. Proof: RCM now represents 29% of healthcare IT spend (~$19B market); Oracle competes on the largest single budget line in health IT.

## TOP OBJECTIONS & RESPONSES

### Objection 1: "Epic has better UX"

**What they mean**: KLAS scores, anecdotal clinician complaints post-upgrade, or Epic demos that show polished consumer-grade interfaces.

**The response**:
"UX scores reflect the legacy Cerner platform, not the Oracle Health EHR we're proposing today. The new system is voice-first. Clinicians don't navigate menus -- they speak to retrieve labs, meds, and records. KLAS tracks historical satisfaction on platforms that will be sunset. Ask us to show you the new platform and then compare. More importantly, ask your physicians: would they rather have a slightly prettier interface or get two hours of their evening back from documentation? Our voice-first ambient AI targets that outcome specifically. Epic's ambient tools are add-ons. Ours are the foundation."

### Objection 2: "Epic has more integrations"

**What they mean**: Epic's App Orchard marketplace has 700+ third-party integrations; concern about Oracle's ecosystem breadth.

**The response**:
"Epic has more historical integrations built over 30 years. Oracle has a better integration architecture for 2026 and beyond. Our semantic AI foundation is FHIR-native, ONC-compliant, and open to third-party AI models and custom agents -- meaning you are not dependent on a marketplace controlled by your EHR vendor. We also have the deepest ERP, supply chain, and finance integrations of any EHR on the market through the Oracle stack -- something Epic App Orchard cannot replicate. And OCI is TEFCA-connected for external data exchange. The question is not how many integrations were built in 2015 -- it is how well your platform connects in 2026 and 2027. That story is Oracle's."

### Objection 3: "Switching costs are too high"

**What they mean**: Budget constraints, implementation risk, workflow disruption, multi-year distraction for clinical leadership -- all real concerns, especially in the current margin environment.

**The response**:
"You are right that switching has real costs. Let us change the frame: you are already paying switching costs every day you run Millennium on aging infrastructure. The question is directed-switching-costs-now versus accumulated-drag-costs-forever. Oracle's Millennium Migration Program is designed to make the intra-Oracle upgrade faster, less expensive, and lower-risk than any competitive replacement. We can show you a side-by-side TCO analysis. Additionally, our AI revenue cycle ROI is calculable within 90 days of go-live -- in many cases, the RCM uplift funds a significant portion of the migration cost. We are not asking you to take a financial risk. We are showing you how to fund this with savings it generates."

### Objection 4: "Oracle support has declined post-acquisition"

**What they mean**: This is a real perception issue. The 2022-2024 Cerner-to-Oracle transition was turbulent. Customers experienced support degradation, roadmap uncertainty, and communication gaps.

**The response**:
"That feedback was earned and we know it. Here is what changed: the new Oracle Health EHR is not a patch on Cerner. It is a new product, built by a new team, under new leadership (Seema Verma). The VA/DoD restart in 2026 is not a proof point we are asking you to take on faith -- it is a federal government that re-evaluated Oracle Health after the turbulence and recommitted. We want to show you our current support SLAs, our dedicated customer success team for your account, and references from health systems that went live in 2025. The 2022 acquisition story is not the 2026 Oracle Health story. Let us earn back the relationship with current evidence, not promises."

## WIN THEMES THIS CYCLE

**Theme 1: "AI-Native vs. AI-Added"**
Oracle built its EHR with AI at the core. Competitors added AI tools to existing platforms. The architectural difference matters for reliability, workflow coherence, and long-term capability expansion. Use the voice-first demo as the visceral proof. The question for buyers: "Would you rather have AI bolted on, or AI baked in?"

**Theme 2: "The Full-Stack Premium Is an ROI, Not a Cost"**
Health systems running Oracle EHR + Oracle DB + OCI + Oracle Analytics + Oracle ERP have one vendor, one support contract, one data governance framework, one AI infrastructure. The savings in integration maintenance, security licensing, and vendor management are quantifiable. Competitors want buyers to see the Oracle stack as expensive. Oracle should want buyers to see it as consolidation -- and consolidation has margin.

**Theme 3: "Federal-Grade Security for Commercial Healthcare"**
The VA/DoD Oracle Health deployment is the most demanding healthcare IT environment in the world: scale, security, interoperability, data complexity. Commercial health systems that run Oracle Health inherit that engineering. No other commercial EHR vendor has been asked to perform at federal scale and passed the test. In a world where 84% of CIOs are increasing cybersecurity budgets by a median 26% in 2026, this heritage is a substantive differentiator.

## COMPETITIVE TRAPS THEY'LL SET

### Trap 1: "Epic's 10-Year Roadmap Presentation"

**What Epic does**: Epic's sales team runs extended roadmap sessions showing a 10-year innovation pipeline with hundreds of AI projects, deeply detailed integration maps, and a reference network of happy customers.

**The trap**: It makes Oracle's roadmap look sparse by comparison and positions any launch timeline uncertainty (e.g., acute care EHR timing) as disqualifying.

**The counter**: Do not compete roadmap-vs-roadmap. Flip the frame to architecture. "Epic's roadmap is impressive -- but it is built on infrastructure that will require every one of those features to be re-engineered as they try to modernize the underlying platform. Oracle's roadmap is shorter because we are not carrying 30 years of technical debt. We are shipping AI-native capability now, not promising to retrofit it later. Ask Epic when their voice-first clinical agent will ship natively -- not as a bolt-on from an acquisition."

### Trap 2: "Total Cost of Ownership Comparison That Buries Oracle"

**What they do**: Epic's financial team prepares a TCO model showing Oracle'smigration + license + support costs on one side versus Epic's full-service implementation on the other, often using selective assumptions (e.g., Oracle's highest-cost implementation tier, Epic's most favorable contract terms).

**The trap**: The numbers look like they favor Epic and put Oracle's team in a defensive, data-matching position throughout the rest of the deal.

**The counter**: Get ahead of it with your own TCO model before Epic presents theirs. Oracle's should include: (1) Millennium status-quo cost if no action is taken, (2) revenue cycle savings from AI denial prevention and coding accuracy, (3) infrastructure consolidation savings from OCI vs. managed third-party data centers, (4) reduced integration licensing from Oracle full-stack vs. Epic + Azure + point solutions. Oracle wins the TCO comparison when the full Oracle stack is the comparison unit -- not just the EHR in isolation.
