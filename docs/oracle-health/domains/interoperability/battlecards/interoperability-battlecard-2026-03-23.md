**1. The only EHR vendor with a built-in QHIN — live, free, and zero-setup for existing customers.**
Oracle Health Information Network received its TEFCA QHIN designation in November 2025. Existing Oracle Health customers activate via the Connection Hub with an opt-in — no consulting engagement, no separate contracting, no new configuration. Every other FHIR data exchange competitor requires organizations to build, broker, or buy their TEFCA connection separately. Oracle includes it.

**2. TEFCA activation from inside the EHR workflow — not bolted on from outside.**
Oracle Health Seamless Exchange aggregates, cleanses, and deduplicates patient data from all connected networks and delivers it directly into the Oracle Health Foundation EHR and the next-gen EHR. No separate portal. No data hop. Clinical staff see complete patient records in the tool they already use. This is operationally superior to any "middleware" or "cloud FHIR layer" approach.

**3. Interoperability is the fuel source for the Oracle Clinical AI Agent.**
Oracle's Clinical AI achieves >40% documentation reduction for clinicians — but only because the underlying data foundation is complete. A FHIR API that goes nowhere doesn't power AI. Oracle's QHIN → Seamless Exchange → Clinical AI Agent is a closed-loop value chain. Ask any competitor to show an equivalent end-to-end. They cannot.

**4. Oracle has already solved the TEFCA transition that is breaking every other vendor's roadmap.**
Epic forced its entire customer base onto TEFCA by end of 2025. Carequality has aligned with TEFCA SOPs. CommonWell is migrating infrastructure to ELLKAY. Oracle anticipated this shift and secured QHIN designation before the race fully materialized. The field is set — and Oracle is already on it.

**Your response**: "Epic's network is the largest — and it is optimized for Epic customers. If your entire ecosystem is Epic, they have an argument. **Proof point**: Oracle QHIN connects to the full TEFCA ecosystem. Epic's exchange prioritizes Epic participants.

## WINNING DISCOVERY QUESTIONS

**Question 1**: "What is your current strategy for the January 1, 2027 CMS Prior Authorization API requirement — and where are you in the implementation timeline?"

Why it works: Opens the urgency conversation without being pushy. Either they have a plan (and you can evaluate if Oracle fits faster/better) or they don't (and Oracle becomes the solution).

**Question 2**: "Are you currently connected to TEFCA through a designated QHIN — and if so, how much does that cost you on top of your EHR contract?"

Why it works: Forces disclosure of any separate QHIN contracting cost, which Oracle can immediately undercut (Oracle QHIN is free for existing customers). If they're not connected to TEFCA, the 500M records exchanged nationally becomes an immediate gap-identification moment.

**Question 3**: "When your clinicians need a complete out-of-network patient history — say, from another state or a non-affiliated hospital — what does that workflow actually look like today, and how long does it take?"

Why it works: Grounds the conversation in operational pain. If the answer involves faxes, phone calls, manual record requests, or waits of more than 24 hours, Oracle Seamless Exchange directly solves the problem they just described. Do not wait for them to connect the dots — connect them for them.

## LANDMINES

**CommonWell naming confusion.**
Do not lead with "CommonWell" as Oracle's interoperability story. CommonWell is migrating its infrastructure to ELLKAY. If customers ask, acknowledge: "CommonWell was an important milestone we helped build. Oracle Health Information Network (our QHIN) is the next generation of that vision — built on OCI, TEFCA-compliant, and available now."

Why: Customers who recall Oracle/Cerner's CommonWell leadership may assume Oracle still fully owns and operates CommonWell. The ELLKAY migration creates confusion. Stay clean: always use "Oracle Health Information Network" and "Oracle QHIN."

**Azure API for FHIR (if prospect uses it).**
Do not attack Microsoft or position Oracle as anti-Azure. Many health systems use Azure for non-clinical workloads and will not appreciate vendor criticism.

Why: The retirement of Azure API for FHIR is a legitimate migration trigger. Acknowledge it neutrally and help the prospect think through migration options — one of which is OCI + Oracle Health infrastructure.

**"Cerner legacy" references.**
Avoid referencing the Cerner brand unless the prospect brings it up in context of their own history. The Oracle Health rebrand is intentional; reinforcing the Cerner association can trigger legacy perceptions around complex implementations.
