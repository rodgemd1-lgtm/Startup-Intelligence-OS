## WHY ORACLE WINS IN HEALTHCARE ERP

**1. Only vendor with native ERP + EHR integration under one cloud.**
No competitor offers this. Workday, SAP, and Infor are ERP-only. Oracle is the only vendor where clinical data (from Oracle Health/Cerner EHR) flows directly into supply chain demand planning, workforce scheduling, and financial reporting. When a CFO asks "why is my OR supply cost spiking?" — Oracle can answer from within one system. Proof: Billings Clinic-Logan Health and Children's Hospital LA are deploying Oracle ERP *and* Oracle EHR simultaneously (December 2025).

**2. Purpose-built healthcare SCM — with no third-party gaps.**
Oracle's supply chain is complete: warehouse management, transportation management, contract management, procurement, sourcing — all native, all included. Workday requires Tecsys for WMS, Cargoson for TMS, and acquired-and-incomplete tools for contracts. This matters in healthcare: medical device recall management, 340B split billing, pharmaceutical tracking, and clinical supply alignment require end-to-end native capability. Proven results: 50% decrease in slow-moving inventory, 40% improvement in forecast accuracy, 35% reduction in procure-to-pay time (Oracle Value Realization Benchmark, Feb 2026).

**3. Agentic AI included — Workday charges extra.**
Oracle embeds agentic AI, AI Agent Studio, SCM AI Agents, and optimized LLMs in the base platform — no surcharges. Workday charges additional fees for AI agent usage (limited to HiredScore in HCM context). In a healthcare market where 80% of execs face board pressure for immediate AI ROI (KPMG), a platform that includes AI at no extra cost wins in procurement. This is not marketing — it's provable in the contracting phase.

**4. Federal government trust: Oracle OCI runs CMS.**
In February 2026, Oracle Cloud Infrastructure was selected to support the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services modernization initiative. For CFOs evaluating cloud risk, this is the single most powerful proof point for cloud security and healthcare data governance. No competitor can claim the federal healthcare payer as a customer reference.

## TOP OBJECTIONS & RESPONSES

### Objection 1: "Workday is better for HR/workforce."
**Response:**
Workday is very good at HR for general enterprises. But healthcare workforce is fundamentally different — physician credentialing, nurse scheduling by certification and unit, PRN pool management, float pool optimization across facilities, per-diem cost tracking, and clinical licensure compliance. Oracle HCM includes healthcare-specific workforce management that integrates directly with clinical operations. Workday's HR is generalist; Oracle's HCM is healthcare-native.

**The flip:** Ask them — has Workday ever integrated their scheduling system with an EHR for census-driven staffing? Oracle has. With UPMC and Northwell as documented case studies. Would that data integration matter for your staffing cost management?

**Proof point:** Oracle HCM for healthcare includes credentialing, scheduling, and direct integration with Oracle Health EHR for acuity-based staffing. Workday has no equivalent EHR integration.

---

### Objection 2: "We're on PeopleSoft and we don't want to change."
**Response:**
You're already an Oracle customer, which makes this less a change and more an upgrade. PeopleSoft is in continuous maintenance mode — Oracle is investing in Oracle Fusion Cloud, not PeopleSoft. Here's the truth: every new Oracle innovation (agentic AI, cloud analytics, integrated SCM, EHR connectivity) lives in Fusion Cloud, not PeopleSoft. Staying on PeopleSoft means running on yesterday's platform while your competitors are running on tomorrow's.

**The positive:** Oracle knows your contract, your data model, your integrations. A PeopleSoft-to-Oracle-Cloud migration is lower risk than any competitor replacement — and Oracle provides dedicated migration tools, Oracle Cloud Manager, and a structured upgrade path. One documented healthcare system reduced AP processing costs by up to 82% after migrating with GenAI automation.

**Close:** Would you rather migrate to Oracle Cloud (where you already have a trusted relationship) or re-evaluate the market and potentially land on Workday — starting from scratch, no EHR integration, and no SCM?

### Objection 3: "SAP has more modules."
**Response:**
SAP has more modules for *manufacturing* and *general enterprise* — that's their core market. In healthcare, SAP's story just changed dramatically: they are discontinuing IS-H, their healthcare-specific ERP product, and handing off their hospital template to a third-party consulting firm (Snap Consulting). 60+ hospitals in DACH are in product transition right now because SAP pulled back.

Oracle is investing in healthcare — not retreating from it. Oracle has healthcare-specific modules for pharmaceutical procurement, medical device recall management, 340B compliance, No Surprises Act billing compliance, and clinical supply chain alignment. And Oracle is the only ERP vendor to also offer a clinical EHR. SAP cannot match this.

**Close:** When SAP's strategy is to exit healthcare-specific solutions, is that really the vendor you want to double down on for the next 10 years?

These are not connector integrations — they are architected data flows within Oracle's cloud. Is your concern about a specific integration point? Let's bring in our Oracle Health technical team to map the exact ERP-to-EHR workflow for your use case.

**Note**: Acknowledge that Oracle is still standardizing the joint go-to-market across Oracle Health (formerly Cerner) and Oracle Cloud ERP teams — but the product integration roadmap is real and accelerating.

## WINNING DISCOVERY QUESTIONS

**Question 1 — Expose Supply Chain Pain (and Workday's Gap):**
"When a medical device recall happens, how long does it take your team to identify every affected device across all your facilities, PAR locations, and care settings? And can you link that recall directly to patient encounters in your EHR?"

*Why it works*: This question is unanswerable without either Oracle's integrated SCM+EHR or a very expensive patchwork of systems. Workday cannot answer this question at all — they have no native recall management and no EHR. Use the silence to position Oracle's precision recall management + clinical data linkage.

**Question 2 — Expose the Cost Pressure Urgency:**
"Strata Decision Technology's latest data shows health system supply expenses up 12.3% year-over-year. How much visibility does your current system give you into where those costs are actually going — and can you link supply spend to specific care line performance?"

*Why it works*: This positions you as informed, data-driven, and aligned with the CFO's current pain. It naturally leads to Oracle's service line profitability analytics and integrated financial reporting.

**Question 3 — Expose the PeopleSoft/Infor/SAP Migration Risk:**
"Your current platform — what year did you implement it, and when was the last major capability upgrade? When your team needs a new AI-enabled workflow, how long does that typically take to get on the roadmap?"

*Why it works*: For PeopleSoft, Lawson, and SAP IS-H customers, this question surfaces the platform stagnation that is already happening. Oracle Fusion Cloud receives quarterly updates with new AI features. Legacy platforms get patches. The contrast is stark once they start thinking about their last 3 years of platform innovation.

## LANDMINES

**Landmine 1: Oracle ERP and Oracle Health go-to-market coordination**
*Topic to avoid*: Deep discussion of exactly how Oracle Cloud ERP and Oracle Health (Cerner) sales teams coordinate, share accounts, and co-sell.
*Why*: The organizational integration between the Oracle ERP group and the Oracle Health (formerly Cerner) group is still maturing post-acquisition. If a buyer probes "one Oracle" execution and you don't have a clean answer, it undermines the integration value prop. *Play*: Keep at product level — the integrations exist at the platform level, and bring in Oracle Health technical or clinical leadership for deep integration discussions.

**Landmine 2: Oracle Fusion Cloud implementation complexity and timelines**
*Topic to avoid*: Detailed timelines or cost estimates for full Oracle Fusion Cloud healthcare implementations without a dedicated implementation team present.
*Why*: Oracle Fusion Cloud implementations at large health systems (500+ beds) are multi-year projects. Competitors (especially Workday) will use timeline fear to create urgency. Don't volunteer complexity; instead, redirect to phased implementation approaches and reference implementations already completed.

**Landmine 3: Gartner ratings (Cloud ERP for Service-Centric Enterprises)**
*Topic to avoid*: Head-to-head Gartner star ratings comparison (Workday 4.5 stars vs. Oracle 4.3 stars in Cloud ERP for Service-Centric Enterprises, 2026).
*Why*: Workday has a slight edge in raw Gartner peer review scores in this category. Redirect to Gartner Source-to-Pay (where Oracle is Leader and Workday is absent) and ISG Healthcare ERP Buyers Guide (Oracle named Leader).

## COMPETITIVE TRAPS THEY'LL SET

### Trap 1: "Show us the Gartner Magic Quadrant where Oracle leads for healthcare ERP specifically"
**The Trap**: Competitors (especially Workday reps) will ask you to produce a Gartner MQ where Oracle is in the top-right for healthcare ERP as a standalone category. Such a specific category may not exist.
**Counter**: "Gartner doesn't publish a Healthcare ERP-specific Magic Quadrant. What they do publish is the Source-to-Pay Suites Magic Quadrant — where Oracle is a Leader and Workday is absent. They also publish Cloud ERP for Service-Centric Enterprises, where both Oracle and Workday appear. The more relevant validation for healthcare specifically is the ISG Healthcare ERP Buyers Guide, which names Oracle a Leader, and KLAS — where our customers have earned recognition across multiple categories. What specific capability are they questioning Oracle's leadership on? Let's go there directly."

### Trap 2: "Workday is already at nine of our peer health systems — we'd rather standardize on what everyone else is using"
**The Trap**: Social proof / herd behavior. Workday's KLAS dominance creates a perception of "safe choice" in healthcare IT procurement. Buyers use this to avoid defending an Oracle selection to their board.
**Counter**: "Workday is strong at the HR and finance layers — and their KLAS wins reflect that. But 'same as peers' means same capabilities, same constraints, same gaps. The health systems differentiating on supply chain efficiency, cost-of-care reduction, and clinical integration are moving to platforms that do more than HR+Finance. Children's Hospital LA, Providence St. Joseph, and Indiana University Health didn't choose Oracle because everyone else was doing it — they chose it because they needed a platform that could handle the full operational complexity of their system. What capabilities are you trying to close on that your current platform doesn't support?"
