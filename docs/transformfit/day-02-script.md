# Day 2: First Real Workout

## Overview
The user is 24 hours into the relationship. If they completed Day 1, they have one small win and a fragile streak — leverage it. If they skipped, they ghosted once but they're still here, which means something. Day 2 introduces the first "real" workout — slightly more than Day 1, still very completable. The coach is getting marginally more direct, still warm. This is the day the user starts thinking "okay, this person actually knows me."

## Behavioral Mechanics at Work

**Sunk cost + endowment effect (Branch A):**
Opening with "That [workout name] you did yesterday" makes the Day 1 rep feel owned. They already have something — the streak is an asset they don't want to lose.

**Implementation intention:**
"After your [morning anchor], you'll..." creates a specific trigger-behavior link. Vague intentions fail; if-then plans are 2-3x more likely to execute (Gollwitzer, 1999).

**Progressive overload signal:**
Day 2 is measurably harder (more reps, longer duration) but still clearly completable. The user feels growth without feeling broken.

**Personalization anchoring:**
The post-workout question ("How do you feel right now?") isn't small talk — the coach explicitly names it as "the data I need to calibrate tomorrow." The user learns their feedback matters.

**No-guilt branch for skippers (Branch B):**
"Life moves fast" is an empathy buffer before accountability. Starting with guilt triggers defensive disengagement. Starting with understanding keeps the channel open.

**Variable reward setup:**
Evening message mentions "something I want to tell you tomorrow" without saying what. Mild anticipation is a variable reward precursor — they'll be more likely to check in morning.

---

## Retention Risk

**Risk:** They completed Day 1 but Day 2 feels harder than expected and they quietly drop off.
**Mitigation:** The "How'd it feel?" opening normalizes any response. "Harder than expected" gets an explicit acknowledgment and recalibration promise. The coach doesn't shame the difficulty — they use it.

**Risk (Branch B):** Skipping Day 1 feels like failure, so they write off the whole program.
**Mitigation:** "That's fine. You're still here" reframes the skip as neutral. The clean-start framing removes the deficit they'd be starting from.
