# Day 5: Streak Stakes

## Overview
It's Friday (or whenever the user's Day 5 lands). They're in a weekend mindset — mentally loosening, schedule shifting. Most apps lose users here because they don't read the room. The coach does. Today's session is shorter by design, the tone is lighter, and the win is getting the user to name a specific time on Saturday they'll move. The Week 1 streak becomes real today — not gamified, just named. And the Week 1 milestone gets seeded as something worth waiting for.

## Behavioral Mechanics at Work

**Streak Protection (Freya)**
The streak is named conversationally, not quantified as a badge or number. "Five days. Every single one." The coach sees it, not the app. This framing keeps it human rather than gamified — which matters because gamification triggers a different psychological response (extrinsic motivation) than relationship-based accountability (intrinsic motivation). Users who feel *seen by a coach* churn less than users chasing streaks.

**Implementation Intention (Freya)**
"What time on Saturday are you thinking about moving?" is not a suggestion — it's a commitment device. Implementation intentions (if-then plans anchored to a specific time and context) are among the highest-efficacy behavior change tools in the literature. By naming the time and having the coach "record" it, the user has made a social commitment they're more likely to honor.

**Reciprocity (Freya)**
The coach has been giving for five days: workouts, insight, the unexpected mid-day message. Day 5 is the first day the coach *asks* for something back — the deeper why answer on Day 4 and the Saturday time commitment on Day 5. Reciprocity is a fundamental behavioral driver. The user has been receiving; they're primed to give back, which deepens investment.

**Anticipation Architecture (Steve)**
"By the end of Day 7, I want to show you something." This is a cliffhanger. Not a gamification mechanic — a relational promise. The coach is saying: I've been paying attention, and I'm going to reflect it back to you. This plants a specific, personal reason to show up on Day 7. The user now has something to look forward to that is *about them*, not about a badge or reward.

**Adaptive Tone (Coach)**
Three branches at the morning check-in handle the full distribution of Friday energy states. Users who say "I actually feel good" get a real workout. Users who are low-energy get a modified session framed as equally valid. Users who say they're busy get negotiated down to something doable rather than lost entirely. This prevents the binary dropout that kills most fitness apps: if the user can't do the "full" workout, they do nothing.

**Loss Aversion Light Touch (Freya)**
"A lot of people start Week 1 and don't finish it." This is a gentle social proof + loss framing without being manipulative. The user is already finishing Week 1; this just makes them aware of the contrast, which increases the perceived value of what they've done.

## Retention Risk

**Primary risk:** Weekend drift. Friday is the last structured day. Saturday and Sunday have no natural "show up" moment, and user behavior patterns change completely. If the user doesn't move on Saturday, the streak breaks and they're statistically more likely to not return Monday.

**Mitigation:**
- The Saturday implementation intention creates a pre-committed plan with a specific time
- "Either way — Sunday is the one that counts" reduces the stakes of Saturday while keeping Sunday salient
- The Week 1 milestone teaser gives the user a specific reason to engage on Day 7 that isn't workout-completion but revelation-anticipation

**Secondary risk:** The user feels pressure from the streak and starts to resent it. The coach explicitly defuses this: "I'm not going to make a big deal out of it — streaks can feel like pressure." This inoculates against the backlash that over-gamified apps create.

**Tertiary risk:** The shorter/optional Day 5 workout feels like the coach is giving up on them. Mitigated by the coach naming the deliberate strategy: "I'd rather you do something than nothing" and framing the lighter session as intentional design, not lowered standards.

## Setup for Next Day

Day 5 plants three Day 6 (Weekend) seeds:
1. **The specific Saturday time** — the implementation intention becomes a check-in prompt. "It's [time they gave] on Saturday. How are you doing?" Already scheduled.
2. **"Sunday is the one that counts"** — creates a secondary anchor so if Saturday drifts, Sunday still has stakes
3. **The Week 1 milestone promise** — "By Day 7, I want to show you something." Day 7 now has a specific, personal reason to exist beyond "completing the week."

The coach also carries forward the Day 4 "deeper why" answer — referenced in the evening teaser as evidence that the coach has been listening across the whole week.

## Metrics Target

**Retention target:** 80%+ users confirm some form of movement on Day 5 (workout or walk counts)
**Commitment target:** 70%+ users provide a specific Saturday time when asked
**Anticipation target:** 65%+ users tap the Day 7 teaser message (signals forward engagement)
**Primary optimization:** Saturday implementation intention rate — users who name a specific time are 2–3x more likely to complete a Day 6 session than users who don't
