## Purpose

Capture structured, actionable competitive intelligence from industry trade shows and conferences (HIMSS, ViVE, Lake Nona, etc.) using a repeatable, ethical methodology that maximizes signal quality while respecting industry norms.

---

## Scope

Applies to all Oracle Health M&CI-attended conferences where competitive intelligence gathering is an objective. Covers:
- Pre-event planning and targeting
- Onsite collection methodology
- Post-event synthesis and distribution
- Deliverable production for executive leadership

## PHASE 1: PRE-EVENT PLANNING (2-4 Weeks Before)

### 1.1 Define Intelligence Priorities

Before every conference, establish 2-4 top-level intelligence questions. These drive all collection activity.

**Example (HIMSS 2026):**
1. How are companies pricing their agentic AI products?
2. What RCM competitive intelligence can we gather?
3. What "aha" ideas emerge about Oracle's strategic direction?

### 1.2 Build Target List

Create a prioritized list of booths and sessions to visit, organized by:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Company** | Vendor name |
| **Booth #** | Floor location |
| **Priority** | P1 (must visit) / P2 (if time) / P3 (opportunistic) |
| **Intel Focus** | Which priority question(s) this target addresses |
| **Known Context** | What we already know about this competitor |

**Source for target list**: Exhibitor directory, conference agenda, existing competitor profiles, Matt's direction, current market signals.

### 1.3 Assign Team Roles (Dynamic — Based on Intelligence Priorities)

Team roles are NOT hardcoded. They are derived from the intelligence priorities identified in Step 1.1. Jake assists this process by:

1. **Analyzing the conference** — exhibitor list, session agenda, keynote themes, sponsor tiers
2. **Mapping to Oracle Health interests** — which conference themes intersect with Oracle's strategic priorities, competitive blind spots, and active deals
3. **Identifying collection lanes** — each intelligence priority becomes a collection lane with an assigned lead

**Process:**
1. Jake ingests the conference exhibitor list, agenda, and marketing materials
2. Jake produces a **Conference Intelligence Brief** with:
   - Top themes relevant to Oracle Health
   - Priority competitors attending (with booth #s)
   - Recommended intelligence questions per theme
   - Suggested collection lanes based on the themes (not predetermined roles)
3. Mike reviews and approves the lane assignments
4. Each lane gets a lead, a question bank, and a target list

**Example (HIMSS 2026) — lanes derived from conference analysis:**

| Lane | Why This Lane | Key Questions |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Pricing & Packaging** | Agentic AI pricing models unsettled across industry | "How does this usually get packaged in the first contract?" / "What's the smallest commercial entry point?" |
| **AI Agents Reality Check** | Every vendor claiming "agents" — need to separate theater from product | "When you say agent, what actions can it take autonomously today?" / "What's live vs preview?" |
| **GTM & Buyer Motion** | Oracle needs to understand how competitors are landing deals | "Who is usually the economic buyer?" / "When this loses, why?" |
| **Proof-Point Validation** | Claims at conferences are inflated — need to pressure-test | "How many live customers in production today?" / "What implementation pain is underestimated?" |
| **Ecosystem / Partner** | Hyperscaler plays + partner-dependent models emerging | "Which partner relationships open deals?" / "What capability gaps do partners fill?" |

These lanes will differ conference to conference. A CHIME event might have lanes for CIO priorities and governance. A J.P. Morgan Healthcare Conference might have lanes for M&A signals and revenue models.

### 1.4 Jake's Conference Intelligence Brief (Pre-Event Deliverable)

Before every conference, Jake produces a **Conference Intelligence Brief** containing:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Event Overview** | Name, dates, location, expected attendance, Oracle's presence |
| **Theme Analysis** | Top 5-7 conference themes mapped to Oracle Health strategic relevance |
| **Priority Target List** | Competitors attending, booth #s, P1/P2/P3 ranking with rationale |
| **Recommended Collection Lanes** | 3-5 lanes with assigned questions, derived from theme analysis |
| **Session Watchlist** | Keynotes and breakouts most likely to surface competitive intelligence |
| **Known Intel Gaps** | What we don't know going in — focuses collection effort |

This brief is the foundation for all onsite activity. Mike reviews, adjusts lanes and priorities, and assigns team members to lanes.

### 1.5 Prepare Capture Materials

- Distribute the **Booth Capture Template** (Section 3.2) to all attendees
- Share the **Two Universal Questions** for quick conversations:
  1. "How are customers typically getting started with this today — what are they buying first, how is it packaged, and where do they usually expand next?"
  2. "On the AI side, what's actually live in production today versus coming soon, and which buyer/use case is driving the fastest adoption?"
- Backup third: "When this wins or loses in a deal, what usually makes the difference?"
- Brief the team on ethical collection rules (Section 5)

## PHASE 2: ONSITE COLLECTION

### 2.1 Two-Pass Booth Sweep (Mike's Methodology)

**Pass 1: Rapid Reconnaissance (7 minutes per booth)**
- One opener question
- Maximum 3 questions
- Collect all public artifacts (brochures, one-pagers, pricing sheets)
- Assign initial confidence score
- Note: voice memo on phone is the fastest capture method between booths

**Pass 2: Targeted Deep Dive (return visits)**
- Go back ONLY to top-priority competitors
- Use the deeper, role-specific question bank (Section 1.3)
- Triangulate findings with:
  - Partner booths (what do their partners say about them?)
  - Customer speakers (are customers confirming the vendor's claims?)
  - Session Q&A (what questions are attendees asking?)
  - Implementation staff (what's the real deployment experience?)

### 2.2 Three-Bucket Classification

Force every vendor claim into exactly one bucket:

| Bucket | Definition | How to Identify |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Sold Now** | GA product being purchased today | Named customers, pricing available, contract terms discussed |
| **Piloted Now** | In beta/preview with real customers | "Select customers," "early access," no public pricing |
| **Roadmap** | Announced but not deployable | "Coming soon," "planned for," no customer references |

**This is where the truth leaks out.** Reps will try to blur these lines. Pin them down.

### 2.3 Red Flags to Watch For

- Won't distinguish GA vs preview vs roadmap
- "Agent" really means UI assistant or copilot
- Savings claims with no denominator, timeframe, or baseline
- Pricing described only as "flexible"
- No clear answer on who buys first
- Strong demo, weak answer on integration and approval workflow
- Repeated partner dependence for core functionality

### 2.4 Beyond the Booth

Best intel often comes from outside booth conversations:

| Source | What to Capture |
|--------|----------------|
| **Keynotes & sessions** | Strategic direction signals, product announcements, market framing |
| **Customer panels** | Real adoption stories, pain points, what they wish vendors did better |
| **Hallway conversations** | Informal market sentiment, rumors, hiring signals |
| **Partner booths** | How they position relative to their partners, integration depth |
| **Analyst / investor sessions** | Market sizing, competitive positioning from neutral parties |
| **Social media / press releases** | Announcements timed to the conference |

## PHASE 3: CAPTURE & DOCUMENTATION

### 3.1 Collection Protocol (Matt Cohlmia's Framework)

All attendees capture notes using these fields:

| Field | What to Capture |
|-------|----------------|
| **Competitor / Organization / Speaker** | Who you talked to |
| **Where** | Session title, meeting type (booth chat / session / public doc) |
| **Observation** | 1-3 bullets: what was said or shown |
| **Artifacts** | Photo of public slide/collateral if permitted; links; session name |
| **"So What"** | Impact on Oracle positioning / accounts |
| **Confidence** | Low / Med / High |

### 3.2 Booth Capture Template (Mike's Field Card)

After every booth visit, complete one entry:

| Field | What to Capture |
|-------|----------------|
| **Vendor** | Company name |
| **Offering** | What they say it is |
| **Buyer** | Who signs / who champions |
| **Land Motion** | Pilot, module, enterprise, services-heavy, etc. |
| **Packaging** | Platform, seat, encounter, transaction, bundle |
| **AI Maturity** | Copilot / Agent / Orchestration / Autonomous action |
| **Proof** | Live customers, named references, metrics |
| **GTM Angle** | Primary wedge and message |
| **Risks** | What sounded weak, vague, or inflated |
| **Confidence** | High / Medium / Low |

### 3.3 Submission Deadline

All notes and photos submitted to shared folders by **Friday of conference week**.
- Photos → shared drive (Photos folder)
- Notes → shared drive (Event Notes folder)

### 4.2 Deliverable Format (Matt's ViVE Template)

The final conference intelligence report follows this structure:

**Section 1: Event Overview**
- Conference name, dates, attendance, Oracle's presence
- 1-paragraph executive summary

**Section 2: Key Themes** (3-7 themes)
- Each theme: statement + evidence + "so what" for Oracle
- Prioritized by strategic impact

**Section 3: Priority Questions Answered**
- Map findings back to the pre-event intelligence questions
- For each question: what we learned, confidence level, remaining gaps

**Section 4: Competitor & Commercial Intel**
- One subsection per competitor visited
- Structure per competitor:
  - Positioning statement
  - Key claims / announcements
  - Evidence quality (named customers, metrics, demos)
  - Implications for Oracle
  - Follow-up actions

**Section 5: Ecosystem & Partnerships**
- Partner signals, alliance shifts, platform plays
- Customer defection risks / save opportunities

**Section 6: Implications & Recommended Actions**
- Specific recommendations tied to evidence
- Urgency classification (act now / monitor / park)
- Owner assignments for follow-up

## PHASE 5: ETHICAL GUIDELINES

### 5.1 Rules of Engagement

**DO:**
- Only capture publicly available information: session content, booth messaging, public brochures, publicly posted pricing/packaging, public press releases
- Follow conference/booth rules on recording and photography
- Use smart, operational, benchmarking-style questions that let people volunteer truth naturally
- Identify yourself honestly if directly asked who you work for

**DO NOT:**
- Pretend to be a buyer if you're not
- Imply purchase intent just to get pricing
- Hide your employer if directly asked
- Ask for confidential customer info, contract terms, or non-public pipeline
- Record or log anything that seems confidential or non-public — if someone shares something confidential, **stop and don't log it**

### 5.2 SCIP Code of Ethics Alignment

This methodology aligns with the Strategic & Competitive Intelligence Professionals (SCIP) ethical standards:
- All information gathered from publicly available sources
- No misrepresentation of identity or intent
- Transparent about organizational affiliation when asked
- No solicitation of confidential information

## PHASE 6: WAR ROOM PROTOCOL (SCIP Best Practice)

### 6.1 Daily On-Site Debrief

Run a daily team sync during multi-day conferences:

| Time | Activity | Duration |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Morning (15 min)** | Set daily collection priorities, adjust target list based on yesterday's findings | 15 min |
| **Evening (30 min)** | Share intelligence, identify cross-cutting patterns, flag surprises | 30 min |

### 6.2 War Room Rules
- Everyone shares their top 3 observations
- Identify any "prediction misses" — where a competitor behaved differently than expected
- Adjust next-day priorities based on emerging themes
- Capture everything — even observations that seem minor can become significant in synthesis

### 6.3 Post-Event Hot Wash (Within 48 Hours)

Before the synthesis begins, run a 60-minute structured debrief:
1. **Round-robin**: Each collector's top 3 findings (5 min each)
2. **Pattern identification**: What themes cut across multiple competitors? (10 min)
3. **Surprise check**: What did we NOT expect to see? (10 min)
4. **Gap analysis**: What intelligence questions remain unanswered? (10 min)
5. **Action items**: What requires immediate follow-up vs. ongoing monitoring? (10 min)

## INDUSTRY BENCHMARKS & MATURITY

### CI Event Maturity Model (Synthesized from Gartner, Forrester, SCIP)

| Level | Description | Characteristics |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **1 — Ad Hoc** | No plan, no process | People grab brochures, come home with business cards, no synthesis |
| **2 — Reactive** | Some collection, no structure | Individual reps report anecdotes, no coordinated effort |
| **3 — Defined** | Plan exists, roles assigned | Pre-event KIQs, assigned collectors, post-event debrief |
| **4 — Managed** | Systematic with metrics | Repeatable process, ROI tracked, competitor profiles updated |
| **5 — Optimized** | Strategic, AI-augmented | Real-time capture, AI synthesis, feeds into war gaming and strategic planning |

**Oracle Health M&CI current level: 3-4** (defined process via this SOP + automation via Jake)

### Key Industry Stats
- Companies that systematically analyze competitor activities at events are **2.3x more likely to outperform peers** in market share growth (Gartner, 2024)
- Allocate **5-10% of trade show budget** to CI activities (RivalSense industry benchmark)
- Structured team debrief should happen within **2-4 weeks** of event (SCIP standard)
- No major consulting firm (McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte) publishes a dedicated trade show methodology — **this SOP exceeds published industry standards**
