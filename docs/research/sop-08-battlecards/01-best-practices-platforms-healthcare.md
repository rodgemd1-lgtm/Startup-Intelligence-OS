Cards without proof points had significantly lower rep usage.\n\n#### Klue — Know-Say-Show Framework\nSource: [Klue — Know, Say, Show](https://klue.com/blog/battlecard-best-practices)\n\nA content strategy layer sitting above FIA:\n- **Know** — Intelligence reps need before the conversation (CI awareness layer)\n- **Say** — Exact positioning language and talk tracks\n- **Show** — Supporting evidence: case studies, G2 reviews, analyst quotes, benchmarks, certifications\n\n#### Crayon — ABC Framework\nSource: [Crayon — ABCs of Battlecards](https://www.crayon.co/blog/how-to-write-a-better-battlecard-follow-the-abcs)\n\nContent quality standard:\n- **Accurate** — Every claim sourced and datestamped\n- **Brief** — Reps access battlecards 3 minutes before or during a call. Word economy is mandatory, not a preference.\n- **Contextual** — Content must match deal stage: early = quick dismiss, late = objection handling + pricing\n\n#### SCIP's Battlecard Development Requirements\nSources: [SCIP Foundational Tools](https://www.scip.org/page/Competitive-Intelligence-Foundational-Tools-and-Practices); [CompeteIQ — SCIP Virtual Battlecard Workshop](https://www.competeiq.io/2020/06/30/scip-virtual-battlecard-workshop/)\n\nSCIP (25,000 members, 120 countries) positions battlecard development as requiring:\n- **Sales needs and workflow analysis before content creation** — Build what reps need in-call, not what PMM thinks is important\n- **Industry tiering** — Not all competitors warrant identical battlecard depth\n- **Customer persona analysis** — Battlecard content should differ by buyer type (CIO vs. CNO vs. CFO in health IT)\n- Content validated against sales effectiveness metrics, not internal confidence\n\n#### Klue's Five Anchor Questions\nSource: [Klue Battlecards 101](https://klue.com/blog/competitive-battlecards-101)\n\nEvery battlecard must answer:\n1. How do I know who I'm competing against?\n2. How do I differentiate?\n3. How do I attack?\n4. How do I defend?\n5. How does this competitor approach the market?\n\n---\n\n### 2. Content problem** — Cards are too long, too marketing-flavored, or not tied to actual deal dynamics. They answer questions reps do not have and miss the questions reps actually face. Fix: FIA framework. Know-Say-Show. Verified proof points mandatory. Rep input during content creation, not after.\n\n**2. Freshness problem** — Cards are treated as one-time deliverables instead of living systems. Reps stop trusting cards that have been wrong once. Trust erodes faster than it builds. Fix: Named ownership, 45-day floor, automated trigger monitoring, rep feedback loops.\n\n**3. Distribution problem** — Cards exist somewhere reps do not go during deals. This is the most common failure mode. The Klue benchmark gap (34% median adoption, 72% top quartile) is almost entirely explained by distribution architecture, not content quality. Fix: CRM integration, Gong/conversation intelligence surfacing, Slack automation. Go to where reps already are.\n\n### The Health IT Complication\n\nHealth IT battlecards carry three structural burdens absent in typical SaaS programs:\n\n**Regulatory surface area** — Compliance posture, certification status, and government contract history are material buyer criteria. Generic frameworks have no section for this. Building these sections is not optional.\n\n**Deal complexity and timelines** — EHR decisions take 18-36 months. A battlecard from 18 months ago about a competitor's AI capabilities is worthless. Monthly updates are not a best practice in this market; they are a competitive requirement given the pace of Epic's and Oracle Health's product releases.\n\n**Buyer fragmentation** — In a hospital system, the effective buyer is simultaneously a CIO, CNO, CMO, CFO, and IT operations team. A single battlecard serves none of them optimally. Persona-tiered battlecards are a structural requirement in health IT, not an enhancement.\n\n### AI Platform Inflection Point (2025-2026)\n\nAutomated monitoring, AI-generated draft content, and real-time in-call battlecard surfacing are now table stakes, not differentiators. The remaining competitive differentiation is in integration depth with CRM and deal workflow; win/loss data ingestion from call transcripts; and API/MCP access for AI assistant integration (Crayon's MCP Server is the first in class and represents a meaningful direction for AI-native teams). Teams relying on manual battlecard creation and PDF distribution are now operating with a structural disadvantage that compounds over time.\n\n---\n\n## Open Unknowns\n\n1. **No peer-reviewed causal research exists.** All win-rate impact data comes from vendor-reported or survey-based practitioner research.
