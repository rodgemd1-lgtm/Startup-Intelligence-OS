Decision fragmentation.** Buying decisions in healthcare involve clinical champions, C-suite approvers, IT committees, GPO contracts, and in IDN environments, both corporate and regional decision-makers. A \"customer\" is not one person — it is an organizational consensus. Market sizing models that use \"number of hospitals\" as the unit conflate the decision unit.\n\n**2. GPO intermediaries.** 97% of hospitals are affiliated with at least one GPO (Group Purchasing Organization). GPO contracts create preferred vendor relationships that constrain competitive displacement. A product may be technically eligible for 5,000 hospitals but contractually accessible to only the hospitals outside existing GPO agreements.\n\n**3. Sales cycle length.** Healthcare technology sales cycles run 12-24 months, sometimes longer for enterprise EHR. This means a market sizing model built on annual purchase volumes dramatically understates the actual opportunity being evaluated in any given period.\n\n**4. Installed base lock-in.** Epic implementation costs $20M+ for medium teaching hospitals. Switching costs include migration, training, downtime risk, and workflow disruption. The \"addressable\" market in many HIT segments is not the full installed base — it is the subset of organizations whose contracts are expiring or whose current vendor is performing below expectations. KLAS is the only source that directly measures this.\n\n**5. Network effects.** EHR value is partially derived from connected providers being on compatible systems (care coordination, data exchange, referral workflows). This creates a self-reinforcing concentration — Epic's 30%+ hospital market share generates network value that compounds its installed base lock-in.\n\n**6. Regulatory constraints on decision timing.** Certification requirements (ONC), HIPAA compliance reviews, and clinical safety validation gates add 6-18 months to procurement timelines. This means market activity is highly lumpy — concentrated in post-contract-expiry windows.\n\n### The VBC Market Sizing Problem\n\nValue-based care markets have a unique sizing problem: the product does not create revenue for the buyer — it reduces cost. This inverts the standard TAM formula. The correct frame is:\n\n> TAM for a VBC/population health product = addressable waste pool × realistic capture rate × vendor's share of savings\n\nThis requires: (1) a baseline total cost of care per attributed patient, (2) a modeled reduction rate from the intervention, (3) a revenue model (shared savings %, PMPM, or license fee). Synthesis\n\n### What Works\n\n**Bottom-up from installed base** is the most defensible method for health IT. Start from AHA facility count or CMS NPI count, segment by type, multiply by estimated spend per unit, cross-validate against known vendor revenues.\n\n**KLAS replacement velocity** is more actionable than TAM for vendors entering established markets. If 8% of hospitals switched EHR vendors last year, that replacement pool is the real near-term market — not the full installed base.\n\n**CMS NHE categories are the only legitimate top-down anchor.** Using $4.8T total U.S. healthcare spend as a TAM for a health IT product is not methodology — it is theater. Use the relevant service category.\n\n**MEPS is underused** for bottom-up technology market sizing. Per-condition, per-patient spending data enables sizing of disease-specific clinical workflow markets.\n\n### Why Healthcare Market Sizing Fails More Than Other Industries\n\nSix structural factors compound the difficulty:\n\n**1. Decision fragmentation.** A \"customer\" is not one person — it is an organizational consensus involving clinical champions, C-suite approvers, IT committees, GPO contracts, and (in IDNs) both corporate and regional decision-makers. Models that use \"number of hospitals\" as the unit conflate the decision unit.\n\n**2. GPO intermediaries.** 97% of hospitals belong to at least one GPO. GPO preferred vendor relationships constrain competitive displacement regardless of product quality. A product may be technically addressable to 5,000 hospitals but contractually accessible only to those outside existing GPO agreements.\n\n**3. Sales cycle length.** 12-24 month sales cycles mean annual purchase volumes dramatically understate the opportunity being evaluated in any given period.\n\n**4. Installed base lock-in.** Epic costs $20M+ to implement at a medium teaching hospital. Switching costs (migration, training, downtime, workflow disruption) mean the addressable market in most HIT segments is not the full installed base — it is the subset of organizations whose contracts are expiring or whose vendor is performing below expectations. KLAS is the only source that directly measures this pool.\n\n**5. Network effects.** EHR value is partially derived from connected providers being on compatible systems. Epic's 30%+ hospital market share generates network value that compounds lock-in. This means simple \"facilities × price\" models overestimate penetrability.\n\n**6. Regulatory timing constraints.** ONC certification requirements, HIPAA compliance reviews, and clinical safety validation add 6-18 months to procurement timelines. Market activity is lumpy — concentrated in post-contract-expiry windows — not annually distributed.\n\n### The Syndicated Report Credibility Problem\n\nThe 6.5× spread in RCM market estimates ($51.6B to $343.78B for 2024) is not measurement error — it is market definition disagreement. Some firms include all hospital billing operations; others count only outsourced RCM software revenue. Numbers are not comparable across firms without reading the scope section.\n\n### The VBC Market Sizing Problem\n\nVBC/population health tech does not create revenue for the buyer — it reduces cost. This inverts the standard TAM formula. The correct frame is:\n\n> TAM = addressable waste pool × realistic capture rate × vendor's share of savings\n\nInputs: (1) baseline total cost of care per attributed patient, (2) modeled reduction rate from the intervention, (3) revenue model (shared savings %, PMPM, or license). None of these come from standard market sizing sources.\n\n---\n\n## Open Unknowns\n\n1. **KLAS's TAM production process** — whether they produce independent TAM estimates or license market size from IDC is not publicly documented.\n2. **Gartner's healthcare IT spending forecast decomposition** — the detailed methodology is paywalled.\n3. **Chilmark's specific scoring rubric** — \"evidence-based\" is stated but the report construction process is not public.\n4. **CHIME's data contribution** — referenced alongside KLAS in health IT circles but their market sizing methodology inputs are undocumented publicly.\n5. **Definitive Healthcare technology installation data quality** — accuracy, recency, and coverage rate of their EHR installation records against ground truth is not publicly benchmarked.\n6. **Academic literature gap** — peer-reviewed papers specifically on health IT market sizing methodology (as distinct from adoption research) are sparse.
