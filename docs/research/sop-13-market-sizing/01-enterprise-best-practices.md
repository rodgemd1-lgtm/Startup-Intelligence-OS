Standard Methodologies — What Consulting Firms Actually Do\n\n#### TAM/SAM/SOM (Structural Framework, not a method)\n\nTAM/SAM/SOM is a **framing convention**, not a methodology. The methodology question is how you derive each number. Consulting practitioners — including those from Bain — note that the terminology is not always used explicitly internally; the concepts are applied through structured segmentation.\n\n**Key protocol from McKinsey/BCG practice:**\n- MECE segmentation first: break the market into non-overlapping, collectively exhaustive segments by demographic, usage, geography, or vertical\n- Apply top-down AND bottom-up independently, then reconcile\n- If the two approaches diverge by more than ~20%, the inputs are wrong — investigate before publishing\n- Never rely solely on third-party research reports (Gartner, IDC) — use them as a ceiling check only\n\n#### Bottom-Up Unit Economics (Preferred for B2B Enterprise)\n\n**Protocol:**\n1. Define the Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) with specificity: industry, size, geography, tech stack, regulatory regime\n2. Count the universe of ICP-qualified accounts (use sources: Definitive Healthcare, LinkedIn, CMS data, SEC filings)\n3. Assign realistic Annual Contract Value (ACV) — derived from comp analysis, not aspirational pricing\n4. Multiply: Universe × Win Rate × ACV = SOM; Universe × ACV at 100% penetration = SAM\n5. Apply penetration decay: first 10% of market is easier than the next 10%; model S-curve, not linear\n\n**What makes bottom-up credible:**\n- ICP count comes from a verifiable source, not estimated\n- ACV is benchmarked to actuals (competitor pricing, disclosed deal sizes, customer case studies)\n- Win rate is grounded in pipeline data or comparable company benchmarks\n- Sales capacity math is included: headcount × quota attainment × ramp time = SOM ceiling\n\n#### Top-Down (Structural Check, not Primary Method)\n\n**Protocol:**\n1. Source total addressable spend from an authoritative dataset (CMS National Health Expenditure Data, BLS, Census industry surveys)\n2. Apply narrowing multipliers: segment share × geography share × product category share\n3. Apply value capture rate: the % of total spend that flows to software/services vs. labor, capital, etc.\n4. Cross-check against bottom-up\n\n**Limitation:** Top-down inflates because it counts spend that will never convert to the product in question. Always apply procurement friction discounts (see Pitfalls section).\n\n#### Value Capture Methodology (McKinsey / L.E.K.)\n\nThis method sizes the market by quantifying the economic value the solution creates, then applying an expected value capture rate.\n\n**Protocol:**\n1. Identify the customer's unit economics problem: cost/outcome the solution addresses\n2. Quantify value created per customer: cost savings, revenue lift, risk reduction\n3. Apply willingness-to-pay (WTP) constraint: what % of value created will a customer pay? (Typically 10–30% in enterprise software)\n4. Multiply by addressable customer universe\n5. Cross-check against comp pricing\n\nL.E.K. uses conjoint analysis for WTP estimation in complex product markets. In B2B healthcare, WTP is often bounded by reimbursement structure, not raw value created.\n\n#### Competitor Revenue Benchmarking\n\n**Protocol:**\n1. Map all competitors with disclosed revenues (public filings, Crunchbase, PitchBook, earnings calls)\n2. Estimate revenues for private competitors via: headcount × revenue per employee benchmarks, or ARR multiple × valuation\n3. Sum of all competitor revenues = a floor estimate of the current market\n4. Map the installed base universe — for hospitals, the universe is ~6,200 U.S. acute care hospitals (AHA data); for physicians, ~1.1M active physicians (AMA); for payers, ~900 health plans (CMS)\n2. Assign current EHR/HIT vendor for each segment (Definitive Healthcare, KLAS Research, CMS attestation data)\n3. Segment by: contract vintage (when is renewal?), satisfaction score (KLAS rating), switching propensity (size, system vs. independent, recent M&A)\n4. Greenfield opportunities: new care settings, new modalities not yet automated\n5. displacement market is structurally limited in inpatient\n- Ambulatory is more fragmented — Epic holds 43.9%, Oracle Cerner 25.1% — more competitive\n\n**Payer side (commercial insurers, Medicare Advantage plans, Medicaid managed care):**\n- Size by: covered lives × PMPM (per member per month) pricing benchmarks\n- Regulatory overlay: CMS interoperability rules (FHIR API mandates), state Medicaid contracting cycles, CMS innovation model participation\n- Payer segment is smaller today but growing faster (highest CAGR in health IT market)\n\n#### Regulatory Considerations in Healthcare Market Sizing\n\nRegulations are not just compliance costs — they define and restrict the addressable market:\n\n1. **HIPAA BAA requirement**: limits vendor eligibility to those who can execute Business Associate Agreements — screens out many general-purpose AI/SaaS vendors\n2. **ONC 21st Century Cures Act (information blocking rules)**: creates new market demand for interoperability solutions but also creates compliance burden that slows adoption\n3. **CMS reimbursement linkage**: solutions that can be reimbursed (CPT codes, quality measure credit) have larger addressable markets than those that cannot — the SAM is effectively the set of reimbursable or cost-saving use cases\n4. **State-level variation**: Medicaid programs vary by state; behavioral health parity laws vary; telehealth reimbursement rules vary — always specify whether sizing is national or state-adjusted\n5. **Contract cycle rigidity**: hospital IT contracts run 7–10 years; realistic window for displacement is the 18–24 months before renewal — this is a structural throttle on SOM that top-down methods ignore\n\n#### Ipsos Medical Device/Diagnostic Market Sizing Protocol (Applicable to Health IT)\n\nIpsos uses a four-phase approach for healthcare market sizing:\n1. **Epidemiological baseline**: define the patient/provider/payer universe from clinical/claims data\n2. **Treatment algorithm mapping**: understand current workflow and where the solution fits\n3. **Primary expert research**: interviews with KOLs (Key Opinion Leaders), procurement heads, and payer medical directors to validate penetration assumptions\n4. **Scenario construction**: build Conservative/Base/Aggressive scenarios with explicit epidemiological and adoption assumptions\n\nKey deliverable: a clear assumptions register that documents every assumption used in defining and sizing the market, how competitors will impact the brand, and how/if the product will grow the market.\n\n---\n\n### 3. Quality Standards — What Makes Sizing Credible\n\n#### The Source Tier Hierarchy\n\n| Tier | Description | Trust Level |\n|------|-------------|-------------|\n| **Tier 1** | Primary research: customer interviews, surveys, transactional data, CMS/government datasets | Highest — directly observed |\n| **Tier 2** | Audited company financials, SEC filings, CMS cost reports, peer-reviewed academic studies | High — independently verified |\n| **Tier 3** | Trade association data, KLAS/Gartner/IDC with methodology disclosed | Medium — aggregated but credible |\n| **Tier 4** | General analyst reports without disclosed methodology, press releases, unverified estimates | Low — use as directional only |\n| **Tier 5** | AI-generated market summaries, unattributed website statistics | Unreliable — never use as primary source |\n\nRule: every TAM/SAM/SOM number must trace to at least a Tier 2–3 source. SOM must incorporate at least one Tier 1 input.\n\n#### The Assumption Register\n\nEvery credible market sizing includes an explicit register of:\n- Market definition (what is included and excluded, and why)\n- Universe size source (date, methodology, URL or citation)\n- Pricing/ACV assumption (source: comp pricing, customer interviews, disclosed contracts)\n- Penetration rate assumption (source: analogous market comps, management judgment — label clearly)\n- Value capture rate assumption (rationale for the % of spend that converts to revenue)\n- Growth rate assumption (source, methodology — distinguish CAGR from point-in-time)\n\nThe Ipsos standard: \"The forecast will be meaningless unless delivered with a clear set of assumptions.\"\n\n#### Sensitivity Analysis Protocol\n\nSensitivity analysis is used to identify which assumptions drive the most variance in the market size estimate.\n\n**Standard protocol:**\n1. Identify 3–5 key input variables (e.g., universe size, ACV, penetration rate, value capture rate, growth rate)\n2. Define a range for each: ±10%, ±25%, or based on primary research bounds\n3. Run single-variable sensitivity: change one assumption at a time, hold others constant — produces a tornado chart\n4. Identify the top 2–3 load-bearing assumptions (those that swing the estimate most)\n5. Note these explicitly in the output: \"This estimate is most sensitive to X and Y\"\n\n**What this accomplishes:** Investors and executives can immediately see where to probe. If the estimate is highly sensitive to a penetration assumption that is poorly sourced, that is a flag to fix before publishing.\n\n---\n\n### 4. Common Pitfalls\n\n#### Pitfall 1: TAM Inflation via Overly Broad Market Definition\n\n**What happens:** Define the market as \"digital health\" or \"healthcare IT\" instead of the specific workflow or care setting the product addresses. Then cite a Gartner or Grand View Research headline number.\n\n**Why it's wrong:** The TAM is the set of customers who could plausibly buy the product in its current form — not all buyers in an adjacent space.\n\n**Fix:** Start with the ICP definition, not the market report. Work outward to TAM, not inward from TAM.\n\n#### Pitfall 2: Double Counting Across Segments or Geographies\n\n**What happens:** Divide the market by vertical (e.g., \"healthcare companies\") AND by company size (e.g., \"companies with 500+ employees\") — then sum both without deduplication. Large health systems appear in both buckets.\n\n**Fix:** Use MECE segmentation. Verify that segment definitions are mutually exclusive before summing. Use a universe database (Definitive Healthcare, Dun & Bradstreet) to count unique accounts, not category combinations.\n\n#### Pitfall 3: Ignoring Procurement Friction\n\n**What happens:** The model assumes linear, frictionless adoption. It ignores: contract cycle timing, compliance/legal review, IT integration complexity, budget allocation cycles, procurement approval chains.\n\n**Why it matters:** B2B healthcare deals can take 9–18 months from first meeting to signature. Even if a customer wants the product, they cannot buy it this fiscal year. This constrains SOM dramatically.\n\n**Fix:** SOM must include a deal cycle discount. For healthcare enterprise, a standard discipline is to apply: realistic annual deal capacity (quota × sales headcount × conversion rate) as a ceiling on SOM, independent of total market size.\n\n#### Pitfall 4: Confusing Revenue with Spend\n\n**What happens:** Use total healthcare IT \"spend\" data (which includes labor, hardware, infrastructure, and legacy maintenance) as the TAM for a software product.\n\n**Why it's wrong:** Total spend ≠ addressable software revenue. The value capture rate (what % of total spend flows to the solution in question) is often 5–15%, not 100%.\n\n**Fix:** Always apply a value capture rate. Document the rationale. In enterprise software, the typical range is 10–30% of the economic value created; in infrastructure/commoditized categories, lower.\n\n#### Pitfall 5: Using Global Numbers Without Local Constraints\n\n**What happens:** Quote a global market size (e.g., \"$45B global digital health market\") when the product is only viable in markets with specific regulatory, reimbursement, or infrastructure conditions.\n\n**Fix:** Always specify geography. Apply country-level or region-level constraints: HIPAA applicability (U.S. only), GDPR compliance requirements (EU), local language/localization requirements.\n\n#### Pitfall 6: Equating Users with Buyers\n\n**What happens:** Count all potential users (nurses, physicians, patients) as the addressable market. But users do not procure — the decision is made by hospital IT, CFO, or payer medical director.\n\n**Fix:** Size the market by the buying unit, not the using unit. In healthcare: the hospital system or IDS (Integrated Delivery System), not the individual clinician. In payer: the health plan, not the member.\n\n#### Pitfall 7: Static Assumptions in Dynamic Markets\n\n**What happens:** Use a single-point estimate with no growth rate methodology, or apply a uniform CAGR from a research report without validating whether the drivers of that CAGR apply to the specific segment.\n\n**Fix:** Decompose the growth rate. Identify the actual drivers (new regulations creating demand, clinical evidence building, reimbursement pathway opening, competing legacy system end-of-life). Model each driver separately.\n\n---\n\n### 5. Scenario Modeling — Conservative/Base/Aggressive\n\n#### Structure of Three-Scenario Analysis\n\nThe three scenarios are not just \"low/mid/high\" — each represents a coherent, internally consistent story about how the world develops.\n\n| Scenario | Definition | Input Approach |\n|----------|-----------|----------------|\n| **Conservative** | Most challenging plausible outcome — not catastrophic, but unfavorable. Regulations slow adoption, competition intensifies, procurement friction is high. | Use low end of research-supported ranges for all key inputs |\n| **Base** | Most likely outcome — reflects current trajectory, historical trends, management plan. | Use midpoints or probability-weighted averages |\n| **Aggressive** | Most favorable plausible outcome — new regs create tailwinds, major competitor exits, faster-than-expected adoption. | Use high end of research-supported ranges for all key inputs |\n\n**Key discipline:** Each scenario must be plausible. \"Aggressive\" is not fantasy — it is a coherent story about favorable conditions materializing. If aggressive requires assumptions that have no historical precedent, it is not a scenario — it is a hope.\n\n#### Probability Weighting Protocol\n\nAfter defining three scenarios, assign probabilities that sum to 100%. Standard consulting convention:\n- Conservative: 20–30%\n- Base: 50–60%\n- Aggressive: 15–25%\n\nThen compute: Expected Value = Σ (Scenario Market Size × Probability)\n\nThis converts scenario analysis into a single defensible point estimate with explicit uncertainty acknowledgment.\n\n#### What Each Scenario Must Include\n\nFor each scenario, document:\n1. **Market definition**: same across all three (do not change the market you are sizing)\n2. **Universe size**: may vary if regulatory expansion or contraction is a driver\n3. **Penetration rate**: the primary driver of scenario divergence in most B2B analyses\n4. **ACV/pricing**: may vary if competitive pressure or value-based pricing applies\n5. **Timeline**: the year(s) the sizing applies to — scenarios often diverge further out in time\n\n#### Phoenix Strategy Group Five-Step Scenario Protocol\n\nA documented five-step process for new market scenario modeling:\n1. Define the market boundaries (geography, customer segments, product scope)\n2. Identify scenario drivers — the 3–5 variables that most determine which scenario materializes\n3. Standard Methodologies — What Consulting Firms Recommend\n\n#### TAM/SAM/SOM: A Framing Convention, Not a Method\n\nTAM/SAM/SOM is structural framing, not a methodology. Practitioners at Bain note the terminology is not always used internally — the concepts are applied through MECE segmentation. The methodology question is *how* each number is derived.\n\n**The McKinsey/BCG consensus protocol:**\n- MECE segmentation first: segment by demographic, usage, geography, or vertical — segments must be non-overlapping and collectively exhaustive\n- Run top-down AND bottom-up independently, then reconcile\n- If the two methods diverge by more than ~20%, the inputs are wrong — investigate before publishing\n- Never use third-party research reports (Gartner, IDC) as primary sources — use them as ceiling checks only\n\n#### Bottom-Up Unit Economics (Preferred Method for B2B Enterprise)\n\nThis is the method consulting practitioners and investors consistently prefer over top-down:\n\n1. Define the Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) with specificity: industry, size, geography, tech stack, regulatory regime\n2. Count the universe of ICP-qualified accounts using a verifiable source (Definitive Healthcare, LinkedIn, CMS data, SEC filings, AHA data)\n3. Assign a realistic ACV benchmarked to actuals — competitor pricing, disclosed deal sizes, customer case studies — not aspirational pricing\n4. Multiply: Universe × Win Rate × ACV = SOM; Universe × ACV at full penetration = SAM\n5. Apply penetration decay: the first 10% of market penetration is faster than the next 10%; model an S-curve, not linear growth\n\n**What makes bottom-up credible:** ICP count from a verifiable source; ACV benchmarked to actuals; win rate grounded in pipeline data or comparable benchmarks; sales capacity math included as a SOM ceiling (headcount × quota × ramp time).\n\n#### Top-Down (Structural Check, Not Primary Method)\n\n1. Source total addressable spend from an authoritative dataset (CMS National Health Expenditure Data, BLS, Census industry surveys)\n2. Apply narrowing multipliers: segment share × geography share × product category share\n3. Apply a value capture rate — the % of total spend that flows to software/services, not labor or capital\n4. Cross-check against bottom-up\n\n**Limitation:** Top-down inflates because it counts spend that will never convert to the product in question. Always apply procurement friction discounts.\n\n#### Value Capture Methodology (McKinsey / L.E.K.)\n\nL.E.K. uses this extensively, particularly for pricing and packaging strategy:\n\n1. Identify the customer's unit economics problem — the cost or outcome the solution addresses\n2. Quantify value created per customer: cost savings, revenue lift, risk reduction\n3. Apply willingness-to-pay (WTP) constraint — typically 10–30% of value created in enterprise software; bounded by reimbursement structure in healthcare\n4. L.E.K. uses conjoint analysis for WTP estimation in complex product markets\n5. Multiply value capture per customer × addressable universe; cross-check against comp pricing\n\n#### Competitor Revenue Benchmarking\n\n1. Map all competitors with disclosed revenues (public filings, Crunchbase, PitchBook, earnings calls)\n2. Estimate private competitor revenues via: headcount × revenue-per-employee benchmarks, or ARR multiple × valuation\n3. Sum of all competitor revenues = a floor estimate of the current market\n4. Add white space: unsold potential and expansion adjacency estimates\n5. Apply HHI to characterize concentration — high concentration means realistic SAM is structurally more constrained\n\n---\n\n### 2. Healthcare-Specific Market Sizing Methodology\n\n#### The Core Difference: Installed Base Analysis\n\nIn healthcare IT, the market is not primarily greenfield. The critical question is not \"how big is the total market\" but \"how much of the installed base is switchable, on what timeline, and at what cost.\"\n\n**Protocol:**\n1. Map the installed base universe: ~6,200 U.S. acute care hospitals (AHA); ~1.1M active physicians (AMA); ~900 health plans (CMS)\n2. Assign current EHR/HIT vendor for each segment using: Definitive Healthcare, KLAS Research, CMS Promoting Interoperability attestation data (public and free)\n3. Segment by: contract vintage (when is renewal?), satisfaction score (KLAS rating), switching propensity (independent vs. system, recent M&A activity)\n4. Identify greenfield opportunities: new care settings, new modalities not yet automated\n5. Identify displacement opportunities: accounts where switching is realistic in a 3–5 year window\n\n**Key data sources:**\n- KLAS Research — vendor satisfaction + market share by segment (paid)\n- Definitive Healthcare — hospital-level data: vendor, bed count, financials (paid)\n- CMS Promoting Interoperability attestation records — public, shows EHR vendor by provider (free)\n- AHA Annual Survey (free summary; detailed data paid)\n- ONC Health IT Dashboard (public, free)\n\n#### Installed Base Reality Check (Health IT 2026)\n\nPer peer-reviewed research (PMC/JAMA) and Definitive Healthcare data:\n- Epic Systems holds 41.3% of hospital installations and 43.9% of ambulatory share\n- Epic + Oracle Cerner collectively hold 71.7% of hospital beds\n- Market has moved from \"competitive\" to \"highly concentrated\" — HHI reflects this\n- Oracle Health holds 25.1% of ambulatory EHR market\n\n**Implication for market sizing:** Any sizing of the inpatient EHR displacement market must account for massive switching barriers. Greenfield sizing (new care settings, AI layer on top of existing EHR) is more defensible than displacement sizing.\n\n#### Provider vs. Payer Segmentation\n\n**Provider side:** Size by number of facilities × beds × applicable ACV OR number of physicians × ACV. Epic/Cerner dominance constrains displacement market severely in inpatient.\n\n**Payer side:** Size by covered lives × PMPM (per member per month) pricing benchmarks. Payer segment has smaller current revenue but highest CAGR in health IT. Key regulatory overlay: CMS interoperability rules (FHIR API mandates), state Medicaid contracting cycles.\n\n#### Regulatory Constraints That Define SAM\n\nRegulations are not compliance footnotes — they structurally bound what is addressable:\n\n1. **HIPAA BAA requirement** — screens out vendors who cannot execute Business Associate Agreements; limits the vendor-eligible universe\n2. **ONC 21st Century Cures Act (information blocking rules)** — creates new market demand for interoperability but slows adoption via compliance burden\n3. **CMS reimbursement linkage** — solutions that generate CPT code reimbursement or quality measure credit have structurally larger SAMs; solutions that cannot be reimbursed rely solely on cost-savings ROI\n4. **State-level variation** — Medicaid programs, behavioral health parity laws, telehealth reimbursement all vary by state; always specify whether sizing is national or state-adjusted\n5. **Contract cycle rigidity** — hospital IT contracts run 7–10 years; realistic displacement window is 18–24 months before renewal. This is a structural throttle on SOM that top-down methods ignore.\n\n#### Ipsos Healthcare Market Sizing Protocol (Applicable to Health IT)\n\nIpsos MDD (Medical Device & Diagnostics) uses a four-phase approach directly applicable to health IT:\n1. Epidemiological baseline: define the patient/provider/payer universe from clinical or claims data\n2. Treatment/workflow algorithm mapping: where does the solution fit in the current care or administrative workflow?\n3. Primary expert research: interviews with Key Opinion Leaders, procurement heads, payer medical directors\n4. Scenario construction: Conservative/Base/Aggressive with explicit adoption and penetration assumptions\n\nKey deliverable standard (Ipsos): \"The forecast will be meaningless unless delivered with a clear set of assumptions\" — including how competitors impact the brand and how/if the product grows the market.\n\n---\n\n### 3. Quality Standards — Credible vs. Hand-Wavy\n\n#### The Source Tier Hierarchy\n\n| Tier | Description | Trust Level |\n|------|-------------|-------------|\n| **Tier 1** | Primary research: customer interviews, surveys, transactional data, CMS/government datasets | Highest |\n| **Tier 2** | Audited financials, SEC filings, CMS cost reports, peer-reviewed academic studies | High |\n| **Tier 3** | Trade association data, KLAS/Gartner/IDC with disclosed methodology | Medium |\n| **Tier 4** | General analyst reports without methodology disclosure, press releases | Low — directional only |\n| **Tier 5** | AI-generated market summaries, unattributed website statistics | Unreliable — never use as a key input |\n\n**Standard:** Every TAM/SAM/SOM number must trace to Tier 2–3 minimum. SOM must incorporate at least one Tier 1 input. If a number cannot be traced to a tier, it is an assumption — label it as such.\n\n#### The Assumption Register (Required Deliverable)\n\nEvery credible sizing includes an explicit register documenting:\n- Market definition: what is included and excluded, and why\n- Universe size: source, date, URL\n- ACV/pricing assumption: source (comp pricing, customer interviews, disclosed contracts)\n- Penetration rate assumption: source, or labeled as management judgment if no external support\n- Value capture rate assumption: rationale for the % of spend that converts to this product's revenue\n- Growth rate assumption: source and methodology — distinguish CAGR from point-in-time sizing\n\n#### Sensitivity Analysis Protocol\n\n1. Identify 3–5 key input variables (universe size, ACV, penetration rate, value capture rate, growth rate)\n2. Define a range for each: ±10%, ±25%, or bounds from primary research\n3. Run single-variable sensitivity — change one at a time, hold others constant — produces a tornado chart\n4. Identify the top 2–3 load-bearing assumptions (those that swing the estimate most)\n5. State explicitly: \"This estimate is most sensitive to X and Y\"\n\n**Function:** Allows investors and executives to immediately see where to probe. A sensitivity to a poorly-sourced penetration assumption is a flag to fix before presenting.\n\n---\n\n### 4. Common Pitfalls\n\n**Pitfall 1 — TAM Inflation via Overly Broad Definition**\nDefining the market as \"digital health\" then citing a Gartner headline number. Fix: Start with the ICP definition, not the market report.\n\n**Pitfall 2 — Double Counting Across Segments**\nSegmenting by vertical AND company size without deduplication. Large health systems appear in both buckets. Fix: MECE segmentation; count unique accounts, not category combinations.\n\n**Pitfall 3 — Ignoring Procurement Friction**\nAssuming linear, frictionless adoption — ignoring contract cycle timing, compliance/legal review, IT integration complexity, budget cycles. B2B healthcare deals take 9–18 months. Fix: Apply a deal cycle discount to SOM; use annual deal capacity (quota × headcount × conversion rate) as a SOM ceiling.\n\n**Pitfall 4 — Confusing Revenue with Spend**\nUsing total healthcare IT \"spend\" as the TAM for a software product. Total spend includes labor, hardware, infrastructure, legacy maintenance. Fix: Always apply and disclose a value capture rate (typically 10–30% of economic value created for enterprise software).\n\n**Pitfall 5 — Global Numbers Without Local Constraints**\nQuoting global market size when the product only works in markets with specific regulatory, reimbursement, or infrastructure requirements. Fix: Always specify geography; apply country-level constraints.\n\n**Pitfall 6 — Equating Users with Buyers**\nCounting all clinicians as the addressable market when procurement decisions are made by hospital IT, CFO, or payer medical director. Fix: Size by the buying unit, not the using unit.\n\n**Pitfall 7 — Static Assumptions in Dynamic Markets**\nSingle-point estimate with no growth rate decomposition. Fix: Identify the actual growth drivers (new regulations, clinical evidence, reimbursement pathway) and model each separately — do not apply a flat CAGR from a research report.\n\n**Pitfall 8 — Confusing AI Market Outputs with Primary Research**\n[Strategex case study] AI-generated market sizing overstated a single market by $33 billion. AI tools aggregate and pattern-match across Tier 4–5 sources; they do not triangulate. Never use AI-generated market estimates as a primary source input.\n\n---\n\n### 5. Scenario Modeling — Conservative/Base/Aggressive\n\n#### Structure\n\nEach scenario is not a number — it is a coherent story:\n\n| Scenario | Story | Input Approach |\n|----------|-------|----------------|\n| **Conservative** | Most challenging plausible outcome: regulations slow adoption, competition intensifies, procurement friction is high | Low end of research-supported ranges |\n| **Base** | Most likely outcome: current trajectory, historical trends, management plan | Midpoints or probability-weighted averages |\n| **Aggressive** | Most favorable plausible outcome: regulatory tailwinds, faster adoption, competitor exits market | High end of research-supported ranges |\n\n**Key discipline:** Aggressive is not fantasy. It must be a plausible scenario with internal consistency — not just inflated penetration numbers with no causal story.\n\n#### Probability Weighting\n\nAfter defining three scenarios, assign probabilities summing to 100%:\n- Conservative: 20–30%\n- Base: 50–60%\n- Aggressive: 15–25%\n\nCompute expected value: Σ(Scenario Market Size × Probability). This converts scenario analysis into a single defensible point estimate with explicit uncertainty.\n\n#### What Each Scenario Must Document\n\n1. Market definition — same across all three scenarios\n2. Universe size — may vary if regulatory expansion/contraction is a driver\n3. Penetration rate — primary driver of scenario divergence in most B2B analyses\n4. ACV/pricing — may vary if competitive pressure or value-based pricing applies\n5. Timeline — scenarios often diverge further out; specify the year(s) clearly\n\n#### Phoenix Strategy Group Five-Step Protocol\n\n1. Define market boundaries: geography, customer segments, product scope\n2. Identify scenario drivers — the 3–5 variables that determine which scenario materializes\n3. Develop distinct input sets for each scenario (not just shift one number)\n4. Build the financial model linking scenarios to revenue/share outcomes\n5. Validate against external benchmarks or analogous market histories\n\n---\n\n### 6. Triangulation\n\n#### When Triangulation Is Required\n\n- Estimate is a key input to a board presentation, investor deck, or strategic investment decision\n- Top-down and bottom-up estimates diverge by more than 20%\n- Primary research is limited and secondary sources vary widely\n- Market is nascent (limited historical data) or highly disrupted (historical data unreliable)\n\n#### Standard Two-Method Protocol\n\n**Method 1 (Top-Down):** Authoritative spend data → segment narrowing → value capture rate → TAM → apply penetration for SAM/SOM\n\n**Method 2 (Bottom-Up):** ICP universe count → ACV → win rate → SOM → scale to SAM/TAM by inverting penetration\n\n**Reconciliation rule:** Agreement within ~20% → use the midpoint and document both methods. Divergence >20% → inputs are wrong; investigate before publishing.\n\n#### Third-Method Options\n\n| Third Method | What It Adds | When to Use |\n|-------------|-------------|-------------|\n| Competitor revenue benchmarking | Floor estimate from observed revenues | When competitors have disclosed or estimable revenues |\n| Customer survey (primary) | Validates ACV and win rate assumptions | When pipeline data is thin |\n| Analogous market analysis | Validates penetration rate via comparable market | When market is nascent |\n| Value-chain analysis | Validates value capture rate | When revenue model is novel |\n\n#### Formal Triangulation Protocol (Kings Research Standard)\n\n1. Collect data from three independent sources (industry report, primary survey, competitor benchmarking)\n2. Identify where they agree — high-confidence zone\n3. Identify where they diverge — uncertainty zone requiring additional research\n4. Report a range, not just a point estimate: \"We size this market at $X–$Y billion, with high confidence in the lower bound and moderate confidence in the upper bound\"\n\n---\n\n## Benchmark Targets\n\n| Dimension | Benchmark |\n|----------|-----------|\n| Top-down vs. bottom-up convergence | Within 20% of each other |\n| Number of independent sources (triangulation) | Minimum 3 |\n| Assumption register completeness | 100% of key inputs documented with source |\n| Sensitivity variables identified | Top 3–5 load-bearing assumptions named |\n| Scenario probability distribution | Sums to 100%; base case ≥ 50% |\n| Source tier for all primary inputs | Tier 1–3 only; no Tier 5 |\n| ACV grounding | Benchmarked to actuals, not aspirational |\n| SOM as % of SAM | Defensible relative to sales capacity math |\n| Healthcare installed base source | Tier 1–2: KLAS, Definitive HC, CMS data |\n| Value capture rate | Disclosed explicitly; 10–30% for enterprise software |\n\n---\n\n## Synthesis\n\n**What the best analysts do (distilled to seven behaviors):**\n\n1. Start with the customer, not the market report — every number traces to a real account, real ACV, real deal cycle\n2. Run two independent methods and reconcile — top-down as ceiling check, bottom-up as primary\n3. Make every assumption visible — source, range, and which inputs are load-bearing\n4. Apply structural constraints specific to the market — in healthcare: installed base saturation, contract cycle timing, reimbursement linkage, regulatory requirements\n5. Build three scenarios around coherent stories — each with internally consistent inputs, not just three versions of one number\n6. Disclose sensitivity — name the two or three assumptions that, if wrong, change the estimate materially\n7. Separate what is known from what is assumed — Tier 1–2 data stated as fact; management judgment labeled as assumption\n\n**The single most common failure mode:** Confusing the market you *want* to serve with the market you *can* serve given actual procurement realities. TAM is inflated by broad category definitions; SAM is inflated by ignoring regulatory, geographic, and capability constraints; SOM is inflated by ignoring deal cycle, switching cost, and sales capacity ceilings. The fix is structural: build market size from the customer up, not from the report down.\n\n---\n\n## Open Unknowns\n\n1. **Firm-specific methodology documentation** — McKinsey, BCG, Bain do not publish internal market sizing playbooks.
