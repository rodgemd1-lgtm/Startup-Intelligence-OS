# Loss-Framed vs Gain-Framed Copy Templates

This document provides ready-to-use copy templates for retention messaging across multiple scenarios. Each scenario includes a BAD version (gain-framed, which is how most products write copy) and a GOOD version (loss-framed, which behavioral economics research shows is approximately 2x more effective for driving action).

The core insight: telling someone what they will get is far less motivating than telling someone what they will lose.

---

## 1. Dormant User Re-Engagement: D3 (3 Days Absent)

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Ready to crush another workout? Your next session is waiting!"
**Email subject:** "Get back on track with a great workout today"
**Email body:** "We have a new workout ready for you. Come back and keep building your fitness journey. You'll feel amazing after!"

**Why this fails:** The user already knows working out feels good. They are not absent because they forgot — they are absent because the immediate cost of effort outweighs the abstract future benefit. Gain framing adds no new information and triggers no urgency.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Your 18-day streak resets tomorrow. 90 seconds keeps it alive."
**Email subject:** "Your flexibility gains are the first to go"
**Email body:** "It's been 3 days since your last session. Here's what the research says: flexibility is the first fitness component to decline, starting around Day 5. Your 6 weeks of mobility work are still intact today. A 90-second stretch preserves them and keeps your streak at 18 days. [One-tap: Start 90-Second Stretch]"

**Why this works:** It quantifies what is at risk (streak, flexibility gains), provides a specific and credible timeline (Day 5), and offers a trivially small action (90 seconds) that prevents the loss. The user is not being asked to gain something new — they are being asked to protect something they already own.

## 2. Dormant User Re-Engagement: D7 (1 Week Absent)

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Miss us? Come back and earn 2x XP this week!"
**Email subject:** "Your bonus XP week starts now"

**Why this fails:** Bonus XP is an artificial incentive that does not address the real cost of absence. It also trains users to wait for promotions before engaging, creating a discount dependency cycle.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Your aerobic base starts declining this week. One session reverses 3 days of loss."
**Email subject:** "What 7 days away costs your fitness"
**Email body:** "One week without training. Here's the honest picture: Your aerobic capacity (VO2 max) begins measurable decline after 10-14 days of inactivity. You're at Day 7 — still in the safe zone, but the window is closing. Your strength remains intact. Your 47 logged workouts and 12 personal records are still here. One session this week resets the clock. Your plan picks up exactly where you left off. [One-tap: Resume My Plan]"

**Why this works:** It uses a credible scientific timeline, references accumulated assets (47 workouts, 12 PRs), creates time pressure without artificial urgency, and offers a frictionless return path.

## 3. Dormant User Re-Engagement: D14 (2 Weeks Absent)

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**Push notification:** "New workouts just dropped! Check out what's new."
**Email subject:** "Exciting new features and workouts inside"

**Why this fails:** A user who has been absent for 2 weeks does not care about new features. They care about whether their past investment is still relevant and whether coming back will feel overwhelming.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Your fitness has declined 8-12% over 2 weeks. But recovery takes half the time. Start today."
**Email subject:** "14 days: what you've lost and what's still yours"
**Email body:** "Two weeks. Here's where things stand: Your aerobic fitness has declined an estimated 8-12%. Your muscular strength is down approximately 5%. Your 14-week consistency streak ended on [date]. But here's what's still intact: Your 52 workout logs, your 12 personal records, your body composition trend data from the past 4 months, and your custom training plan. Research shows most people recover 2 weeks of fitness loss in about 5-7 days of training. Your plan has been adjusted for your current estimated level — no starting over. [One-tap: See My Updated Plan]"

**Why this works:** It acknowledges the loss honestly (building trust), quantifies what remains (endowment effect), provides a hopeful recovery timeline (removing the "too late" excuse), and shows the plan has adapted (reducing re-entry friction).

## 4. At-Risk User Intervention (Usage Pattern Declining)

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**In-app message:** "Try our new advanced training mode! Push your limits with progressive overload tracking."
**Push notification:** "Unlock your next level with our new training features"

**Why this fails:** A user whose engagement is declining does not need more features. They need a reason to protect what they have already built.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**In-app message:** "Your training consistency has dropped from 4x/week to 2x/week over the past 3 weeks. At this rate, you may start losing the aerobic gains you built in January and February. Want us to adjust your plan to a sustainable 3x/week schedule that protects your progress?"
**Push notification:** "Your consistency is trending down. Adjusting your plan to protect your gains. [Review changes]"

**Why this works:** It uses data the user can verify, frames the intervention around protecting existing progress, and offers a concrete solution (plan adjustment) rather than a generic prompt.

## 5. Milestone Celebration with Loss Framing

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**In-app message:** "Congratulations! You hit 100 workouts! Keep going to reach 200!"
**Push notification:** "100 workouts! You're amazing! Keep it up!"

**Why this fails:** It celebrates but creates no forward retention mechanism. The user feels good for 3 seconds and then the moment passes. There is no cost attached to stopping.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**In-app message:** "100 workouts. That's 87 hours of training, 14 personal records, and 23 weeks of consistency. You've built something most people never achieve. Your progress data, workout history, and calibrated plan represent months of investment that can't be replicated anywhere else. What's next? Your plan for workouts 101-150 is ready."
**Push notification:** "100 workouts in the vault. 87 hours. 14 PRs. That's yours forever — as long as you keep going."

**Why this works:** It quantifies the cumulative investment (sunk cost), implies the cost of stopping (can't be replicated), and immediately bridges to the next commitment (101-150). The celebration itself becomes a retention mechanism.

## 6. Subscription Renewal

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**Email subject:** "Renew your subscription and keep accessing premium workouts"
**Email body:** "Your subscription expires in 7 days. Renew now to continue enjoying unlimited workouts, personalized plans, and advanced analytics. Plus, lock in your current rate before prices increase."

**Why this fails:** It lists features the user already knows about. The "price increase" tactic is overused and erodes trust. It frames renewal as a purchase decision rather than a protection decision.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**Email subject:** "Your 8 months of training data goes read-only in 7 days"
**Email body:** "Your subscription renews on [date]. Here's what's in your account: 147 workout logs spanning 8 months. 23 personal records with progression data. A training plan calibrated across 200+ data points to your exact fitness level. 4.2 months of body composition trend data. Your social connections with 12 training partners. If your subscription lapses, your data moves to read-only. You'll be able to view but not add to your history, and your personalized plan will pause. Your training partners will see your status change to inactive. [One-tap: Keep My Account Active]"

**Why this works:** It inventories everything the user owns and specifies exactly what changes if they do not renew. The phrase "read-only" is less aggressive than "you'll lose access" but carries the same weight. Mentioning social visibility adds accountability cost.

## 7. Feature Discovery

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**In-app tooltip:** "New! Try our AI form checker to improve your exercise technique."
**Push notification:** "Exciting update: AI form checking is here!"

**Why this fails:** Users are bombarded with "new feature" announcements. There is no urgency and no personal relevance.

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**In-app tooltip:** "Your last 3 squat sessions show a pattern that could lead to knee strain. Our form checker can flag this in real-time before it becomes an injury that sidelines your training."
**Push notification:** "Your squat form data suggests a correction that could prevent a setback. [Check it in 30 seconds]"

**Why this works:** It connects the feature to something the user might lose (training time due to injury). The feature becomes protective, not additive.

## 8. Social Re-Engagement

### BAD (Gain-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Your friend Sarah just completed a workout! Join her."
**Email subject:** "See what your friends are up to in the app"

**Why this fails:** Knowing a friend worked out is informational, not motivational. It can actually create negative social comparison ("she's doing it and I'm not").

### GOOD (Loss-Framed)
**Push notification:** "Sarah and 2 others in your accountability group logged sessions this week. You're the only one who hasn't. Your group streak ends Friday."
**Email subject:** "Your accountability group is 1 person short of a perfect week"
**Email body:** "Your 4-person accountability group has a 6-week streak of everyone logging at least one session per week. This week, 3 of 4 have checked in. You're the missing piece. The group streak ends Friday at midnight. A single session of any length keeps it alive. [One-tap: Start Quick Session]"

**Why this works:** It frames the user's inaction as causing a loss for others (social cost), creates a specific deadline (Friday), and emphasizes the group streak (collective ownership). The user is not being asked to gain fitness — they are being asked to prevent a shared loss.

---

## Template Design Principles

1. **Specificity beats generality.** "Your 18-day streak" is stronger than "your streak." Numbers create tangible ownership.
2. **Credibility beats hype.** Cite science, data, or the user's own history. Never invent urgency.
3. **One clear action beats multiple options.** Every message should have exactly one CTA that takes under 2 minutes.
4. **Acknowledge the gap.** For D7+ dormancy, pretending the user was not absent feels dishonest. Name the gap, then bridge it.
5. **Recovery framing.** Always pair loss messaging with a recovery path. Loss without hope creates avoidance, not action.
