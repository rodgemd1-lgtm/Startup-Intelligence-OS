Key message
Yes — you can buy data, scrape some data, and use Monte Carlo simulations. But they are not interchangeable. Use licensed/purchased data for production knowledge, scraping only as a narrow exception, and Monte Carlo/synthetic methods for testing, uncertainty, and edge-case coverage. Also: for your use case, don’t start by training your own base LLM. Start with a strong API model, retrieval, prompt chaining, tool use, and a hard eval loop. OpenAI’s agent docs explicitly frame the core workflow as prepare data → chunk → embed → retrieve, and Anthropic recommends prompt chaining for decomposable tasks and structured skills/workflows for complex ones. Direct answer
Purchase data: yes. The FTC notes AI developers obtain training data through several routes, including buying from data brokers, third parties, or rightsholders directly. That said, buying the data does not remove privacy or deletion obligations. In California, residents have rights to know, delete, correct, and opt out of sale/sharing, and the CPPA’s Delete Act adds broker-side deletion-mechanism duties beginning August 1, 2026. Federal Trade Commission+2California DOJ+2
Scrape data: sometimes, but treat it as the risky lane. In the Ninth Circuit, hiQ v. LinkedIn said the CFAA’s “without authorization” concept does not apply to the public sections of public websites, but that does not wipe out copyright, contract, privacy, or restricted-area claims. CNIL says web scraping is not prohibited under the GDPR if you have a lawful basis and safeguards, but you should minimize collection and not scrape sites that oppose automated collection through CAPTCHAs or robots.txt. The UK ICO goes further: under current UK practice, web-scraped personal data for generative AI is high-risk “invisible processing,” and legitimate interests is the only plausible lawful basis it sees today. CA9 Courts+2CNIL+2
Monte Carlo / synthetic data: yes, absolutely — but as a supplement, not your core truth set. NIST describes Monte Carlo analysis as a way to account for uncertainty. OECD says synthetic data can help when real data are scarce or sensitive, but it also warns that synthetic data can introduce bias, degrade accuracy, and cannot fully replace real-world data. For your fitness app, that makes Monte Carlo great for adherence/churn scenarios, pricing/API-cost ranges, support-load spikes, and agent edge cases — not for pretending you have ground-truth human outcomes you do not actually have. NIST+2OECD+2
Copyright is the squishy part. The U.S. Copyright Office says some AI-training uses may be fair use and some may not, and that outcomes are case-specific. It also says the licensing market is still developing and recommends letting that voluntary market continue to develop for now. That is why licensed data is the cleaner production path than broad scraping of copyrighted works. U.S. Copyright Office+1
One nuance that matters: robots.txt is not an authorization mechanism in the internet standard, but regulators still treat it as an important governance signal. So the practical answer is: don’t rely on “robots.txt isn’t law” as your policy. Honor it anyway. IETF Datatracker+2CNIL+2
How to start your own training data / knowledge base
Stop calling all of it “training data.” You need four separate assets:


Knowledge base — source-of-truth docs for retrieval.


Behavior library — gold examples of good prompts, tool calls, outputs, refusals, and escalation behavior.


Eval set — fixed test cases you score every change against.


Simulation set — Monte Carlo and synthetic scenarios for edge cases, load, ambiguity, and sparse conditions.


For governance, give every source a datasheet/data card with at least: source, collection method, license/rights, intended uses, jurisdictions, PII/sensitivity class, retention/deletion rules, freshness, and owner. NIST stresses provenance and IP compliance for training data, and Google’s Data Cards framework explicitly calls for upstream sources, collection/annotation methods, intended use, and decisions affecting performance. OECD+3NIST Publications+3Google Research+3
For personas, do not try to “train a persona into the model” first. Make personas thin and operational: role, goals, allowed tools, stop conditions, output schema, tone, and 5–10 gold examples. Anthropic’s skills guidance is very aligned with that: concise instructions, clear workflows, and testing with real tasks. Claude

Risk log


Dirty provenance risk: the FTC says firms that unlawfully obtained consumer data can be required to delete models and algorithms built from it. Mitigation: source registry, contract warranties, deletion workflow, kill switch for bad corpora. Federal Trade Commission+1


Privacy overreach risk: scraped personal data is treated as high-risk in UK guidance, and CNIL says minimize collection and respect anti-scraping signals. Mitigation: exclude personal/sensitive data by default, honor robots/CAPTCHAs, add deletion and DSAR handling before production. ICO+2CNIL+2


Synthetic-data overuse risk: synthetic data can help, but may bias or degrade performance and cannot replace real data. Mitigation: use it for evals, edge cases, and sparse scenarios; calibrate against real outcomes. OECD+1


Vendor data leakage risk: use commercial settings, retention controls, and disable feedback/reporting paths where appropriate. privacy.anthropic.com+1
