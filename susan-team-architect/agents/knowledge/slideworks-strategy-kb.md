## 1. Barbara Minto — Pyramid Principle

### SCR Structure

Every strategic narrative follows the Situation-Complication-Resolution arc:

- **Situation**: Establish shared context. What does the audience already know and agree on?
- **Complication**: Introduce the tension. What changed, what went wrong, or what is at risk?
- **Resolution**: Deliver the answer. What should we do, and why?

The SCR arc applies at three levels:
1. The entire deck (macro arc)
2. Each major section (section arc)
3. Each individual slide (micro arc)

### MECE Grouping

All arguments, breakdowns, and categorizations must be:

- **Mutually Exclusive**: No overlap between categories. Each item belongs to exactly one group.
- **Collectively Exhaustive**: No gaps. The groups together cover the entire universe of possibilities.

MECE violations are the most common structural failure in executive decks. Test every grouping by asking: "Is there anything that falls between these categories?" and "Is there anything not covered?"

### Governing Thought Hierarchy

The pyramid has four levels, each governed by the level above:

| Level | Element | Rule |
|-------|---------|------|
| 1 | Deck title | States the single governing thought of the entire presentation |
| 2 | Section titles | 3-5 key line arguments that directly support the deck title |
| 3 | Slide titles | Action titles that support their section title |
| 4 | Slide body | Evidence, data, and visuals that prove the slide title |

Every element at level N must directly support the element at level N-1. If it does not, it is either misplaced or unnecessary.

### Top-Down Answer-First

Lead with the conclusion, then provide the supporting evidence. Never build up to the answer.

- The audience should know your recommendation within the first 60 seconds.
- Supporting logic follows the recommendation, not the other way around.
- This is the opposite of academic writing. Executives do not want to follow your journey of discovery.

### Key Line Arguments

Under each governing thought, provide 3-5 main supporting points:

- Fewer than 3 feels thin and unconvincing.
- More than 5 exceeds cognitive load and suggests the grouping is not MECE.
- Each key line argument must be a complete thought, not a topic label.

### The "So What?" Test

Every slide must answer: "So what does this mean for the audience?"

- If a slide presents data without interpretation, it fails the test.
- If a slide describes a situation without connecting it to the decision, it fails.
- Apply recursively: after answering "so what?" once, ask it again until you reach the actionable implication.

## 2. Gene Zelazny — Say It With Charts

### Message-Driven Chart Selection Matrix

Choose chart types based on the message you are communicating, not the data you have:

| Message Type | Recommended Chart | When to Use |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Comparison | Bar chart (horizontal or vertical) | Comparing discrete items or categories against each other |
| Composition | Pie chart (use sparingly) or Stacked bar | Showing parts of a whole; pie only when 5 or fewer segments |
| Distribution | Histogram or Box plot | Showing how data points spread across a range |
| Relationship | Scatter plot or Bubble chart | Showing correlation or clustering between two or more variables |
| Trend over time | Line chart | Showing change over a continuous time period |
| Sequential change | Waterfall chart | Showing how a starting value increases and decreases to a final value |
| Process or timeline | Gantt chart | Showing phases, milestones, and parallel workstreams |

### Chart Title = The Insight, NOT the Topic

The chart title must state the conclusion the audience should draw:

- GOOD: "Revenue expected to double in Year 3, driven by new customer segments"
- BAD: "Revenue Projection"
- GOOD: "Customer churn concentrated in the first 90 days, suggesting onboarding failure"
- BAD: "Churn Analysis"

### One Chart, One Question

Every chart answers exactly ONE question. If a chart attempts to answer two questions, split it into two charts.

- The title tells you the answer.
- The chart proves it.
- The footnote tells you where the data came from.

If the audience has to study the chart to figure out the message, the chart has failed.

## 3. Nancy Duarte — Resonate Framework

### Audience-First Storytelling

Start with the audience's world, not yours:

- What do they care about?
- What keeps them up at night?
- What language do they use?
- What do they already believe?

The presentation is not about you. It is about moving the audience from where they are to where they need to be.

### Contrast Structure

Alternate between "what is" and "what could be" throughout the presentation:

- **What is**: The current reality, with its problems, friction, and cost.
- **What could be**: The future state, with its benefits, outcomes, and possibility.

This alternation creates tension and release, which sustains attention and builds momentum toward the call to action.

### The Sparkline Narrative Arc

The sparkline is the visual shape of the presentation's emotional trajectory:

1. Begin in the audience's current world (what is).
2. Introduce what could be — creating a gap.
3. Alternate between the two, with each cycle raising the stakes.
4. Build toward the climactic "star moment."
5. End with the new bliss — the world after the audience acts.

### The Big Idea

Every presentation must have exactly one Big Idea, expressed as a single complete sentence containing:

1. **Your unique point of view** — what you believe to be true.
2. **What is at stake** — why it matters if the audience does not act.

Example: "We must enter the UK market in Q2 2026 or lose $85M in first-mover advantage to three competitors already in procurement."

### Star Moment

Every presentation has one emotionally resonant peak — the moment that the audience will remember long after the slides are forgotten:

- It can be a dramatic statistic, a customer story, a visual reveal, or a provocative reframe.
- Plan it deliberately. Do not leave it to chance.
- Place it at the climax of the sparkline, just before the call to action.

## 4. McKinsey Firm Graphics Standards

### Action Titles

Every slide title is a complete sentence that states the slide's conclusion:

- Maximum 15 words.
- Maximum 2 lines.
- Active voice.
- Quantitative when possible.
- 18-20pt bold font, consistent across all slides.

Examples:
- "Market share declined 12% in Q3 due to three competitive launches"
- "Consolidating five regional teams into two hubs saves $4.2M annually"

### One Message Per Slide

If the slide title contains the word "and," the slide should be split into two:

- "Revenue grew 15% and margins improved by 3 points" is two slides.
- Each slide makes one claim and provides the evidence for that claim.

### The Titles Test

Read only the slide titles in sequence. If the titles alone tell the complete argument — with no gaps in logic — the deck passes. If they do not, rewrite the titles until they do.

This is the single most important quality test for any strategic deck.

### The 60-Second Rule

If a slide takes longer than 60 seconds to present, it must be redesigned:

- Split it into multiple slides.
- Simplify the visual.
- Remove non-essential elements.
- Sharpen the title.

### Evidence Hierarchy

Every slide follows a strict hierarchy:

| Layer | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Title | States the answer (action title) |
| Body | Provides the proof (charts, data, analysis) |
| Footnotes | Cites the sources |

### Source Attribution

Every data slide must include a source line at the bottom:

Format: `Source: [data source] (year); [your team] Analysis`

No data claim without attribution. No chart without a source. No projection without stated assumptions.

### Approved Slide Structure

All slides follow a four-part layout:
1. **Title** — action title (complete sentence)
2. **Subtitle** — optional qualifier or scope
3. **Body** — visual evidence (chart, table, framework)
4. **Source** — data attribution

## 5. Bain Results Delivery

### Results-Oriented Structure

Start with the result, then explain how you got there:

- The first slide of every section states the answer.
- Subsequent slides provide the supporting evidence.
- The audience should never have to wait for the punchline.

### Key Question Decomposition

Every engagement starts with THE question — the single most important question the client needs answered:

- "Should we enter the UK market?"
- "How do we reduce churn by 30%?"
- "What is our optimal pricing structure?"

### Logic Tree

Decompose the key question into sub-questions until each is directly answerable with data:

```
Key Question: Should we enter the UK market?
  |-- Is the market large enough?
  |     |-- What is the TAM?
  |     |-- What is our realistic SOM?
  |     |-- What is the growth rate?
  |-- Can we win?
  |     |-- Who are the competitors?
  |     |-- What is our differentiation?
  |     |-- Do we have the right product?
  |-- Is it worth it?
        |-- What is the required investment?
        |-- What is the expected ROI?
        |-- What is the payback period?
```

### Fact-Based Persuasion

No claim without data. Every assertion in the deck must be traceable to:

- Primary data (interviews, surveys, experiments)
- Secondary data (market reports, public filings, databases)
- Derived analysis (models, projections with stated assumptions)

### Issue Tree to Answer Pipeline

The full analytical flow:

1. **Issue tree** — decompose the question MECE.
2. **Hypothesis** — form a testable hypothesis for each branch.
3. **Work plan** — define the analysis required to test each hypothesis.
4. **Analysis** — execute the work plan with data.
5. **Answer** — synthesize findings into the recommendation.

## 6. SlideWorks SCR System

### 10-Section Narrative Structure

Every strategic deck follows this backbone:

| # | Section | Purpose |
|---|---------|---------|
| 1 | Executive Summary | Hook with key metrics; state the ask upfront |
| 2 | Background and Context | Set the scene; establish shared understanding |
| 3 | Problem/Opportunity Definition | Create urgency; quantify the cost of inaction |
| 4 | High-Level Solution and Vision | Show the future state; paint the destination |
| 5 | Options Analysis | Show thoroughness; demonstrate alternatives considered |
| 6 | Recommended Solution | Detailed proposal with financials and evidence |
| 7 | Implementation Plan | Show it is doable; timeline, milestones, resources |
| 8 | Risks and Mitigations | Show realism; anticipate objections |
| 9 | Governance and Monitoring | Show control; KPIs, cadence, accountability |
| 10 | Recommendations and Next Steps | Get commitment; specific asks with owners and dates |

### Audience Calibration

Adjust deck length to the audience:

| Audience | Slide Count | Depth |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| C-Suite (CEO, CFO, Board) | 5-10 slides | Decisions and financials only |
| VPs and SVPs | 15-20 slides | Strategy and key evidence |
| Directors and Managers | 30-50 slides | Full analysis and implementation detail |

### Slide Type Catalog

The SlideWorks system includes 271 pre-built slide variations organized by:
- Narrative function (situation, complication, resolution)
- Content type (data, framework, process, comparison, timeline)
- Visual pattern (chart, table, matrix, flow, icon grid)

### 4 Critical Questions Before Starting

Before writing a single slide, answer:

1. **What decision** needs to be made?
2. **Who decides** — name and title?
3. **What is the problem** or opportunity — quantified?
4. **What is the solution** — with evidence?

If any of these four questions cannot be answered clearly, the deck is not ready to be built.

## 7. Action Title Formulas

### The Formula

```
[Insight] + [Evidence]
```

An action title combines a qualitative insight with quantitative evidence in a single sentence.

### Examples

| Bad Title (Topic Label) | Good Title (Action Title) |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Country Overview | Five Tier 1 countries represent 72% of the $270M opportunity |
| United Kingdom | UK primary care framework creates $85M entry point with 4/5 deal probability |
| Product Gap Analysis | Six product gaps must close to capture the international payer market |
| The Ask | Oracle must invest $11-16M over 18 months to capture $270M SOM |
| Customer Segmentation | Three customer segments drive 80% of retention, requiring different engagement models |
| Competitive Landscape | Two competitors have 18-month head start but lack interoperability — our key differentiator |

### The Titles-Only Test

Print only the slide titles in sequence. Read them aloud. If a colleague who has never seen the deck can understand the full argument from the titles alone, the deck passes. If not, rewrite every title that fails.

This test catches:
- Topic labels masquerading as titles
- Missing logical connections between slides
- Arguments that depend on the body content rather than the title hierarchy

## 8. Chart Title Formulas

### The Formula

```
[Key Insight] + [Supporting Data]
```

A chart title states the conclusion and includes the critical number.

### Examples

| Bad Chart Title | Good Chart Title |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Revenue Projection | Revenue expected to double by Year 3, reaching $65M ARR |
| Procurement Calendar | UK procurement window opens Q2 2026 — 8 active RFPs totaling $1.2B |
| Market Share | Market share erosion accelerated in H2, dropping 4 points to 23% |
| Cost Breakdown | Infrastructure costs represent 62% of total spend — 2x the industry benchmark |
| Customer Satisfaction | NPS improved 18 points after onboarding redesign, from 32 to 50 |

### Chart-Title Integrity Rule

The chart PROVES the title. The title STATES the conclusion.

If the chart does not visually and obviously support the title, one of them is wrong:
- Either the title overstates the data (fix the title).
- Or the chart is the wrong visualization for the message (fix the chart).

Never allow a gap between what the title claims and what the chart shows.

## 9. Narrative Blueprint Output Format

The SlideWorks Strategist agent outputs a structured YAML blueprint for every narrative it designs.

### Schema

```yaml
deck:
  big_idea: "one sentence with POV + stakes"
  narrative_arc: SCR
  audience: "title and level"
  decision_needed: "specific ask"
  total_slides: <integer>
  sections:
    - name: "section name"
      arc_position: "situation|complication|resolution"
      slides:
        - id: 1
          section: "situation|complication|resolution"
          action_title: "complete sentence, max 15 words"
          key_message: "the one thing the audience must remember"
          chart_type: "bar|line|scatter|waterfall|gantt|bubble|pie|table|none"
          slideworks_type: "slide type from 271 catalog"
          data_sources: ["file references or data origins"]
          notes: "presenter notes or additional context"
        - id: 2
          section: "situation"
          action_title: "another complete sentence action title"
          key_message: "single takeaway"
          chart_type: "none"
          slideworks_type: "context-setter"
          data_sources: []
          notes: ""
```

### Field Definitions

| Field | Required | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| `big_idea` | Yes | One sentence: your POV + what is at stake |
| `narrative_arc` | Yes | Always SCR (Situation-Complication-Resolution) |
| `audience` | Yes | Decision-maker title and organizational level |
| `decision_needed` | Yes | The specific decision or commitment being sought |
| `total_slides` | Yes | Integer; must align with audience calibration guidelines |
| `id` | Yes | Sequential slide number |
| `section` | Yes | SCR position: situation, complication, or resolution |
| `action_title` | Yes | Complete sentence, max 15 words, active voice |
| `key_message` | Yes | The single thing the audience must remember from this slide |
| `chart_type` | Yes | One of: bar, line, scatter, waterfall, gantt, bubble, pie, table, none |
| `slideworks_type` | Yes | Matched to the 271-type SlideWorks catalog |
| `data_sources` | Yes | Array of file references, database names, or data origins |
| `notes` | No | Presenter notes, caveats, or delivery guidance |

### Validation Rules

The blueprint must pass these checks before it is considered complete:

1. **Titles test**: Reading only `action_title` fields in order tells the complete argument.
2. **MECE check**: Sections do not overlap and collectively cover the full scope.
3. **SCR arc**: The deck moves through situation, complication, and resolution in order.
4. **Audience fit**: `total_slides` matches the audience calibration table.
5. **Big Idea present**: `big_idea` contains both a point of view and stakes.
6. **Decision clarity**: `decision_needed` is specific and actionable.
7. **Source coverage**: Every slide with a chart has at least one entry in `data_sources`.
8. **No topic labels**: Every `action_title` is a complete sentence, not a noun phrase.
